# How to Conduct a Social Media Audit and Setup

A thorough social media audit is the foundation of any reputation management campaign. Before publishing a single post or responding to a single comment, you need to understand the current state of a client's social presence: what exists, what is missing, what is working, and what is actively harming their reputation. This guide walks through the full audit-to-setup process so that social channels become controlled assets rather than unmanaged liabilities.

## Phase 1: Auditing the Existing Social Presence

### Identify All Platforms

Begin by searching for the brand name, personal name, and any known variations across every major social platform. The core platforms to assess are:

- **LinkedIn** (company page and personal profiles of key executives)
- **Twitter/X**
- **Facebook** (business page and any personal profiles that appear in search)
- **Instagram**
- **YouTube**
- **TikTok**
- **Crunchbase**

Do not limit yourself to these seven. Check Pinterest, Reddit, Glassdoor, and any industry-specific platforms that surface in a Google search for the brand name. The goal is a complete inventory.

### Assess Each Platform

For every profile you find, record the following in a spreadsheet:

1. **Profile exists?** Yes/No, and note whether it is owned by the client or an unofficial/impersonator account.
2. **Completeness.** Is the profile fully filled out? Check bio, about section, website link, contact details, profile image, and banner image.
3. **Last activity.** When was the most recent post or update? A dormant profile with a last post from 2019 sends a worse signal than no profile at all.
4. **Follower count.** Record the current number. This establishes a baseline for growth tracking.
5. **Engagement rate.** Look at the last 10-20 posts. Calculate average likes, comments, and shares relative to follower count. Low engagement on a high-follower account suggests purchased followers or irrelevant content.
6. **Sentiment.** Scan comments and replies. Are they broadly positive, neutral, or negative? Flag any recurring complaint themes.

### Determine Platform Relevance

Not every platform matters equally for every client. A B2B technology company will get far more value from LinkedIn and Twitter/X than from TikTok. A consumer brand targeting younger demographics may need Instagram and TikTok as priorities. Map each platform against the client's target audience and industry to produce a priority ranking. Platforms in Tier 1 get active management. Tier 2 platforms get a polished, static presence. Tier 3 platforms may be claimed and secured but not actively managed.

## Phase 2: Setup and Branding

### Consistent Branding Across All Platforms

Once you know which platforms to activate, ensure every profile presents a unified brand image. Consistency builds trust and improves recognition in search results. Standardise the following across all profiles:

- **Display name.** Use the exact same name everywhere. No abbreviations on one platform and full name on another.
- **Bio/about text.** Write a core bio that can be adapted to each platform's character limit. The first sentence should contain the primary keyword or brand descriptor.
- **Profile image.** Use the same high-resolution headshot or logo everywhere. Ensure it renders well at small sizes (most profile images display at 40-80 pixels in feeds).
- **Banner/cover image.** Use a consistent banner that reinforces the brand message. Update it quarterly or when campaigns change.
- **Website link.** Always link back to the primary website or a dedicated landing page.

### Separating Personal and Corporate Profiles

For individual reputation management, keep personal social profiles distinct from corporate ones. An executive's LinkedIn should reflect their professional identity, not serve as a duplicate of the company page. For corporate clients, ensure that employee personal accounts are not being mistaken for official brand channels. Document which profiles are official and which are personal to avoid confusion during the campaign.

## Phase 3: Content Scheduling and Publishing

### Choosing a Scheduling Tool

Use a social media management platform to schedule and publish content at scale. Hootsuite is a strong choice for reputation management campaigns because of its bulk composer feature, which allows you to import a CSV file containing dozens of pre-written posts with scheduled dates and times. This means you can plan an entire month of content in one session and upload it in minutes.

Other options include Buffer, Sprout Social, and Later. The key requirement is the ability to schedule posts across multiple platforms from a single dashboard and to support team collaboration so that content can be reviewed before publishing.

### Recommended Publishing Schedule

Optimal posting times vary by platform and audience, but as a general starting framework for UK/European audiences publishing Monday to Friday only:

| Platform   | Recommended Time | Notes                                      |
|------------|------------------|--------------------------------------------|
| Facebook   | 3:00 PM          | Afternoon engagement peaks                 |
| Twitter/X  | 5:00 PM          | End-of-day browsing window                 |
| LinkedIn   | 8:00 AM          | Early morning professional browsing        |
| Instagram  | 12:00 PM         | Lunchtime scroll behaviour                 |

Avoid posting at weekends unless the client's audience is active then. For reputation management purposes, consistency matters more than volume. Three high-quality posts per week are better than seven mediocre ones.

### Visual Content Prioritisation

Posts with images consistently outperform text-only posts across every platform. Prioritise visual content wherever possible: branded graphics, infographics, team photos, event images, and short video clips. When repurposing blog content or press mentions as social posts, always attach a relevant image rather than relying on auto-generated link previews, which are often poorly formatted.

## Phase 4: Daily Monitoring and Rapid Response

### What to Monitor Daily

Every active social channel must be checked at least once per day for:

- **Comments on posts.** Respond to questions and positive comments within 24 hours. Respond to negative comments or complaints within 2-4 hours.
- **Direct messages.** Unanswered DMs create a poor impression. Acknowledge receipt even if a full answer requires time.
- **Shares and mentions.** Track who is sharing your content and what they are saying about it.
- **Likes and reactions.** Note any unusual spikes (positive or negative) that may indicate viral activity.

### Rapid Response Protocol

When a negative comment or mention is detected, follow this protocol:

1. **Assess severity.** Is this a genuine complaint, a troll, or a factual error?
2. **Acknowledge publicly.** Post a brief, professional response acknowledging the issue within 2-4 hours.
3. **Move offline.** Invite the commenter to continue the conversation via direct message or email to avoid a public back-and-forth.
4. **Resolve and follow up.** Once the issue is resolved, ask the commenter if they would be willing to update or remove their negative comment.
5. **Escalate if necessary.** If the comment is defamatory, involves legal threats, or is part of a coordinated attack, escalate immediately to the campaign lead.

## Phase 5: Monthly Reporting and KPI Tracking

### Key Metrics to Track

Produce a monthly report covering:

- **Follower growth** across each platform (absolute number and percentage change).
- **Engagement rate** (average likes, comments, shares per post divided by follower count).
- **Post volume** (how many posts were published vs. planned).
- **Sentiment summary** (ratio of positive to neutral to negative comments/mentions).
- **Top-performing content** (identify which posts drove the most engagement and why).
- **Response time** (average time to respond to comments and DMs).

### Reporting Template

Use a consistent template each month so that trends are visible over time. Include a one-page executive summary at the top with traffic-light indicators (green, amber, red) for each KPI, followed by detailed platform-by-platform breakdowns. This format allows clients to grasp the overall picture in 30 seconds and dig into specifics if they choose.

### Iterating on Strategy

The monthly report is not just a deliverable for the client; it is the primary input for refining the social strategy. If LinkedIn posts about industry insights are outperforming company news, shift the content mix. If Twitter engagement is flat despite consistent posting, revisit the posting time or content format. Data should drive every adjustment to the social media plan.

## Checklist Summary

- [ ] Audit all social platforms and record baseline metrics
- [ ] Determine platform priority tiers based on industry relevance
- [ ] Standardise branding (name, bio, image, banner, link) across all profiles
- [ ] Separate personal and corporate profiles clearly
- [ ] Set up scheduling tool with bulk upload capability
- [ ] Load first month of content into scheduler
- [ ] Establish daily monitoring routine
- [ ] Define rapid response protocol and escalation contacts
- [ ] Create monthly reporting template
- [ ] Review and adjust strategy monthly based on KPI data
