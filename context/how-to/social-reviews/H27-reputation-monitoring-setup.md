# How to Set Up Reputation Monitoring

Reputation damage compounds quickly when undetected. A negative article published on Monday that goes unnoticed until Friday has five days of unchallenged visibility in search results, five days of potential social sharing, and five days during which journalists, competitors, or disgruntled parties can amplify the message. Effective reputation monitoring ensures early detection of threats and provides the data needed to track positive progress over time. This guide covers how to build a comprehensive monitoring system using both free and paid tools.

## Why Monitoring Matters

Consider this: 91.7% of all Google click-throughs come from page one results. If a negative article reaches page one for a branded search term, it is being seen by the vast majority of people who search for that name. Monitoring Google search results regularly means you can detect when a negative result moves from page three to page two, and take action before it reaches page one where the real damage occurs.

Monitoring also provides the evidence base for reporting to clients. Without systematic tracking, you cannot demonstrate that positive content is rising, negative content is falling, and the overall sentiment landscape is improving.

## Tool Selection

### Free Tools

**Google Alerts** is the most accessible monitoring tool and should be set up for every reputation management client regardless of budget.

- Cost: Free
- Capabilities: Sends email notifications when new content matching your search terms is indexed by Google. Covers web pages, news, blogs, and video.
- Limitations: Does not cover social media. Can miss content that Google indexes slowly. No sentiment analysis. No dashboard or historical tracking.

Google Alerts is a baseline, not a complete solution. Use it as a safety net alongside more capable tools.

### Mid-Range Paid Tools

**Mention** and **Brand24** offer social listening, web monitoring, and basic sentiment analysis at price points suitable for small to mid-size campaigns.

- Cost: Typically in the range of 50-300 per month depending on the plan.
- Capabilities: Track mentions across social media, news, blogs, forums, and review sites. Basic sentiment classification (positive, neutral, negative). Email and in-app alerts. Simple dashboards.

### Enterprise Tools

**Brandwatch** and **Meltwater** are the leading enterprise-grade monitoring platforms.

- Cost: Significant annual contracts, typically in the thousands per year range.
- Capabilities: Advanced Boolean query building for precise mention tracking. AI-powered sentiment analysis. Competitor benchmarking. Historical data access. Custom dashboards and automated reporting. Influencer identification. Crisis detection alerts.

For large-scale or high-profile reputation management campaigns, Brandwatch or Meltwater provide the depth of data and automation needed to manage monitoring efficiently. For smaller campaigns, Google Alerts combined with a mid-range tool and manual SERP checks will suffice.

## Setting Up Google Alerts

Create alerts for the following search terms at minimum:

1. **Exact brand name.** Use quotes: "Company Name"
2. **Key executive names.** "FirstName LastName" for each principal.
3. **Brand name variations.** Common misspellings, abbreviations, and former names.
4. **Product or service names.** If any specific products have reputation exposure.
5. **Brand name + negative qualifiers.** "Company Name" + scam, "Company Name" + complaint, "Company Name" + fraud. These catch early-stage attack content.
6. **Competitor names.** Optional but useful for context. If a competitor suffers a reputation crisis in your client's industry, similar content may follow.

For each alert, configure:

- **How often:** "As-it-happens" for high-priority terms (brand name, executive names), "Once a day" for secondary terms.
- **Sources:** "Automatic" to cover the widest range.
- **Language:** Set to the relevant language(s) for the client's market.
- **Region:** Set to the relevant region(s) or leave as "Any region" for global monitoring.
- **How many:** "All results" rather than "Only the best results" to avoid missing anything.

## Setting Up Brandwatch or Meltwater

If using an enterprise tool, the setup process is more involved but far more powerful.

### Configure Brand Queries

Build Boolean queries that capture all relevant mentions while filtering out noise. Example query structure:

```
("Company Name" OR "CEO Name" OR "Product Name")
AND NOT (job listing OR careers OR "apply now")
```

The exclusion terms prevent job postings and recruitment content from flooding the results. Refine these over the first two weeks of monitoring as you identify common false positives.

### Set Up Sentiment Tracking

Configure the platform's sentiment analysis for your brand queries. Most enterprise tools use AI to classify mentions as positive, neutral, or negative. Review the first 50-100 classified mentions manually to check accuracy and adjust the sentiment model if the platform allows it. Automated sentiment analysis is imperfect but provides a useful trend indicator when tracked over time.

### Configure Competitor Monitoring

Set up parallel queries for 3-5 key competitors. This provides context for your client's monitoring data: is a spike in negative mentions specific to your client, or part of an industry-wide trend?

### Set Alert Thresholds

Define what triggers an immediate alert versus what goes into the daily or weekly summary:

- **Immediate alert:** Mention in a high-authority news source (national press, major industry publication), mention with strongly negative sentiment, sudden spike in mention volume (more than 3x normal daily average).
- **Daily summary:** All other mentions, sorted by sentiment and source authority.

## Manual SERP Monitoring

No automated tool fully replaces manual Google search monitoring. Automated tools track mentions (who is talking about you), but SERP monitoring tracks visibility (what people see when they search for you).

### Weekly SERP Check Process

Every week, perform the following searches in a clean browser (incognito/private mode, logged out of Google, with location set to the client's primary market):

1. **Brand name search.** Record every result on pages 1 and 2 (typically 20 results). For each result, note:
   - Position (1-20)
   - Title
   - URL/source
   - Classification: Positive, Neutral, or Negative
   - Whether this is a new entry or was present last week

2. **Executive name search.** Same process for each key individual.

3. **Brand name + reviews.** Check what appears for "[Company Name] reviews" as this is a common search by prospective customers.

4. **Brand name + negative qualifiers.** Search for "[Company Name] scam" or "[Company Name] complaints" to check whether any attack content is ranking.

Record all of this in a tracking spreadsheet. Over weeks and months, this spreadsheet becomes the definitive record of SERP movement and the primary evidence of campaign progress.

### Monitoring Pages 1-5

While page one captures 91.7% of click-throughs, monitoring pages 2-5 provides early warning. A negative article that appears on page 4 today could reach page 2 next month if it gains backlinks or social shares. By detecting it early on page 4, you have time to create and promote positive content that prevents it from rising further.

## Social Channel Monitoring

### Daily Manual Checks

Regardless of whether you use automated social listening tools, perform daily manual checks on all active social platforms:

- **Check notifications** for comments, mentions, shares, and direct messages.
- **Search the brand name** on each platform to catch mentions that do not tag the official account.
- **Review competitor activity** for any content that may affect your client's industry or reputation.
- **Check review platforms** (Google Reviews, Trustpilot, etc.) for new reviews.

This daily check should take 15-30 minutes per client and can be delegated to a team member with clear escalation instructions.

### Industry Forum Monitoring

Identify the 3-5 most active forums relevant to the client's industry (Reddit subreddits, industry-specific forums, Quora topics) and check them twice per week. Forum threads can rank in Google and can be early indicators of emerging sentiment trends.

## Escalation Process

Not every negative mention requires escalation. Define clear thresholds:

### Business-as-Usual Negativity

- Individual negative social media comments that can be handled by the social team.
- A single negative review on a review platform.
- Negative mentions on low-authority blogs or forums with minimal readership.

**Action:** Handle within the daily monitoring workflow. Respond per the standard protocol. Log and report in the weekly summary.

### Reputation Threat

- Negative article in a high-authority publication (national press, major industry outlet).
- Coordinated negative campaign (multiple posts or articles appearing in a short timeframe).
- Negative content appearing on Google page 1 for a branded search term.
- Viral social media content (rapidly increasing shares/engagement).
- Legal threats or regulatory actions being publicised.

**Action:** Immediate escalation to the campaign lead. Notify the client within 2 hours. Convene a response strategy meeting within 24 hours. Consider whether legal, PR, or content responses are appropriate.

### Defining the Escalation Chain

Document a clear escalation chain for every client:

1. **Monitoring team member** detects the mention and classifies severity.
2. **Campaign manager** is notified for anything above business-as-usual.
3. **Account director** is notified for high-severity threats.
4. **Client contact** is notified per the agreed communication protocol (some clients want immediate notification; others prefer a daily digest).

## Monthly Reporting

Compile all monitoring data into a monthly report covering:

- **Mention volume** (total mentions, broken down by source type and sentiment).
- **Sentiment trend** (month-over-month comparison of positive vs. negative ratio).
- **SERP changes** (any movements on pages 1-2 for key search terms, with positive/negative classification).
- **Review scores** (current average ratings and review volumes on each platform).
- **Notable mentions** (highlight any significant positive or negative mentions).
- **Threat summary** (any escalated incidents and their resolution status).
- **Recommendations** (actions to take in the coming month based on the data).

This report serves both as a client deliverable and as the strategic input for the next month's campaign activities.

- [ ] Build Boolean queries for brand and competitor monitoring

- [ ] Create SERP tracking spreadsheet and perform first baseline check
- [ ] Establish daily social channel monitoring routine
- [ ] Identify and schedule forum monitoring for key industry forums
- [ ] Document the escalation chain and severity classifications
- [ ] Create monthly reporting template
