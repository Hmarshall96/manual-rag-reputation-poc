# How to Pursue US-Specific Content Removals

Content removal in the United States operates under a fundamentally different legal framework than in the UK or Europe. The First Amendment to the US Constitution provides strong protections for freedom of speech and freedom of the press, which means that many of the tools available to European residents -- particularly the Right to be Forgotten -- do not apply in the US. This guide covers the options that are available, their limitations, and the strategies that can be combined to achieve the best possible outcome.

## The First Amendment and Right to Be Forgotten

The Right to be Forgotten, as established by EU and UK law, is not recognised in the United States. US courts have consistently held that the First Amendment protects the publication of truthful information and, in most cases, information that is a matter of public record. This means:

- You cannot compel Google to de-index search results about you based on privacy or "right to be forgotten" arguments in the US
- News organisations are under no legal obligation to remove truthful articles, even if they are outdated or cause ongoing harm
- Public records, including court records and arrest records, are generally considered protected speech

This does not mean that nothing can be done. It means that the available strategies differ from those used in European jurisdictions and typically require a more indirect approach.

## Expungement Laws

Expungement is the legal process of sealing or destroying criminal records. In the US, expungement laws vary dramatically from state to state, and understanding the specific laws in the relevant jurisdiction is essential.

### What Expungement Does

Expungement directs law enforcement agencies to not disclose the expunged records on background checks. In practice, this means:

- The record will not appear on standard criminal background checks
- You can legally answer "no" when asked if you have been convicted of a crime (in most circumstances)
- Law enforcement databases are updated to reflect the sealed status

### What Expungement Does Not Do

Critically, expungement does **not** require press, websites, or third-party platforms to remove articles or content about your arrest or conviction. A news article about your arrest remains protected speech even after the underlying record is expunged. This is one of the most significant practical limitations of expungement for reputation management purposes.

### State-by-State Variation

Expungement eligibility varies enormously across states:

- **Colorado**: allows expungement for many types of crimes, including some felonies. One of the more permissive states.
- **Wyoming**: prohibits expungement entirely. Once a record exists, it cannot be sealed through state courts.
- **Oklahoma**: allows expungement of arrest records but is more restrictive for convictions.
- **California**: allows expungement for most misdemeanours and some felonies under Penal Code Section 1203.4. Also has specific provisions for marijuana-related offences.
- **New York**: limited expungement but offers sealing of certain records under CPL Section 160.59.

You should consult a criminal defence attorney in the relevant state to understand your specific eligibility and the process required.

## Contacting News Agencies for Unpublishing

While news organisations have no legal obligation to remove content, many have established internal policies for handling requests. The reality is that most major news agencies maintain a strict "no unpublishing" policy. However, they may agree to alternative measures.

### What News Agencies May Agree To

- **Publish edits or corrections**: if the article contains factual errors, the publication may issue a correction or update the article.
- **Add a "noindex" tag**: the article remains on the website but is removed from search engine results. This is the same approach used in UK news removal (see H08) and can be highly effective.
- **Make the subject's identity anonymous**: some publications will agree to remove names from older articles, replacing them with generic descriptions. This is more common for articles about minor offences or where the subject was a juvenile.
- **Add an editor's note**: providing context about changed circumstances (e.g., charges were dropped, conviction was overturned) without removing the article.

### Factors News Agencies Consider

When evaluating an unpublishing request, editors typically weigh:

- **Reasons for the request**: what specific harm is the continued publication causing?
- **Conviction status**: was there a conviction, or were charges dropped/dismissed?
- **Expungement status**: has the record been expunged? While this does not obligate removal, it demonstrates that the legal system considers the matter resolved.
- **Age of the article**: older articles are more likely to be considered for modification than recent ones.
- **Whether you were a minor at the time**: publications are generally more willing to anonymise or remove content involving juvenile subjects.
- **Inaccuracies in the article**: any factual errors significantly strengthen your request.
- **Public interest**: is there an ongoing public interest in the information? High-profile cases, elected officials, and matters involving public safety are less likely to be modified.

### How to Make the Request

1. Identify the publication's managing editor or standards editor. Avoid contacting the journalist who wrote the article directly.
2. Write a formal, measured letter explaining your request. Do not threaten legal action.
3. Address the specific factors listed above that support your case.
4. Be realistic about what you are asking for -- requesting a noindex tag or anonymisation is more likely to succeed than requesting full removal.
5. Follow up if you do not receive a response within two to three weeks.

## California-Specific Laws

California has enacted several laws that provide additional tools for reputation management:

### California Eraser Law (SB 568)

This law, effective since 2015, allows minors (under 18) to request removal of content they personally posted online. Key points:

- Applies only to content posted by the minor themselves, not content posted about them by others
- Requires website operators and app providers to provide a mechanism for minors to request removal
- Does not require removal if the content was posted by someone else, if the minor received compensation for the content, or if the operator anonymises the content

### CCPA and CPRA

The California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) and its successor, the California Privacy Rights Act (CPRA), provide California residents with data privacy rights that are closer to European standards than any other US state law:

- **Right to know**: you can request that businesses disclose what personal data they have collected about you
- **Right to delete**: you can request that businesses delete personal data they have collected from you
- **Right to opt-out**: you can opt out of the sale or sharing of your personal data
- **Right to correct**: you can request correction of inaccurate personal data

These rights apply to businesses that meet certain thresholds (annual revenue over $25 million, or processing data of 100,000+ consumers, or deriving 50%+ of revenue from selling consumer data). They do not apply to news organisations operating under journalistic exemptions, but they can be effective against data brokers, people-search websites, and businesses that aggregate and sell personal information.

To exercise CCPA/CPRA rights, submit a verifiable consumer request to the business, typically through their website's privacy page or by contacting their designated privacy officer.

## Comprehensive US Strategy

Given the limitations of the US legal framework, the most effective approach combines multiple strategies:

### 1. Pursue Expungement Where Eligible

If you have a criminal record that is eligible for expungement in your state, pursue this first. While it does not directly remove online content, it:
- Removes the record from background checks
- Provides leverage when requesting unpublishing from news agencies
- Establishes that the legal system considers you rehabilitated

### 2. Contact News Agencies

After obtaining expungement (or if you are not eligible), approach news organisations with a well-reasoned request. Focus on requesting noindex tags or anonymisation rather than full removal.

### 3. Exercise CCPA/CPRA Rights

For California residents, submit deletion requests to data brokers and people-search sites that are aggregating and displaying your personal information or criminal record data.

### 4. Suppress Through Positive Content

In the US, content suppression through search engine optimisation is often the most reliable strategy. This involves:
- Creating and optimising positive content on authoritative platforms
- Building a strong social media presence across multiple platforms
- Publishing professional content, articles, and thought leadership
- Securing positive media coverage or feature articles
- Developing a personal website optimised for your name

The goal is to push negative content below the first page of search results, where the vast majority of searchers never look.

### 5. Address Data Broker Sites

Numerous websites aggregate public records and personal data in the US (Spokeo, BeenVerified, WhitePages, etc.). Most offer opt-out procedures. Systematically opt out of these services to reduce the spread of negative information. This is a time-consuming but effective process that can be handled manually or through subscription services that automate opt-out requests.

## Key Takeaways

US content removal requires a different mindset than UK or European approaches. The legal tools for compelling removal are far more limited, but a strategic combination of expungement, direct outreach to publishers, exercise of state-level privacy rights, and aggressive content suppression can achieve meaningful improvements. The most successful outcomes typically come from pursuing all available avenues simultaneously rather than relying on any single approach.
