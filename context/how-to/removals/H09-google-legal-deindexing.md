# How to Request Google Legal De-indexing

Google provides several mechanisms for requesting the removal of URLs from its search results on legal grounds. These tools do not remove content from the source website itself, but they can prevent specific pages from appearing when someone searches for your name. This guide covers the main routes available, the information required, and practical tips for maximising your chances of success.

## Understanding De-indexing vs Removal

It is critical to understand the distinction between de-indexing and removal. When Google de-indexes a URL, the page still exists on the original website. Anyone with the direct link can still access it. What changes is that the page no longer appears in Google search results. For most reputation management purposes, this is sufficient, because the vast majority of people discover damaging content through search engines rather than by navigating directly to the source.

De-indexing through Google's legal tools results in a notice appearing at the bottom of affected search results pages: "In response to a legal request submitted to Google, we have removed 1 result(s) from this page." This notice confirms that the de-indexing has been actioned.

## Important Limitation: European Residency

Google's legal removal tools for Right to be Forgotten requests only apply to European country-specific versions of Google (google.co.uk, google.de, google.fr, etc.). They do not apply to google.com. This means that if someone searches using the US version of Google, de-indexed results may still appear. However, Google increasingly geo-targets results based on the searcher's location, so for practical purposes, de-indexing from European versions provides substantial protection for individuals based in the UK or Europe.

## Route 1: Google Legal Troubleshooter

The primary tool for submitting legal removal requests is Google's Legal Troubleshooter, accessible at:

**https://support.google.com/legal/troubleshooter/1114905**

This is a guided form that walks you through identifying the type of legal issue and the Google product affected. For reputation management, the most commonly used path is:

1. Select "Web Search" as the Google product
2. Select the relevant legal issue (defamation, privacy, court order, etc.)
3. Complete the required fields

The Web Search form is the most widely used route and covers the broadest range of legal removal scenarios. It allows you to report URLs that appear in Google's organic search results and request their de-indexing.

## Route 2: Google Outdated Content Tool

If content has already been removed from the source website but still appears in Google's search results or cache, use the Google Outdated Content Tool:

**https://www.google.com/webmasters/tools/removals**

This tool is specifically designed for situations where the page no longer exists (returns a 404 error) or where the content on the page has been significantly changed since Google last crawled it. It is not a legal tool per se, but it accelerates the process of getting Google's index to reflect the current state of a page.

Use this tool when:
- A website has agreed to remove content and has done so, but Google still shows the old version
- A page has been taken down entirely but still appears in search results
- A cached version of a page shows outdated or removed content

## Route 3: Report Other Legal Removal Issue

For cases that do not fit neatly into the standard troubleshooter categories, Google provides a more detailed form under "Report other legal removal issue." This is the most comprehensive submission route and requires the following information:

### Required Information

- **Country of residence**: the country where you are legally resident. This determines which data protection and defamation laws apply.
- **Your full name**: the name as it appears in the content you want de-indexed.
- **Company name**: if the request relates to a business rather than an individual.
- **Contact email address**: Google will use this to communicate their decision.
- **Allegedly infringing URLs**: the specific URLs you want de-indexed. List each one individually.
- **Explanation of why the content is unlawful**: this must cite specific legal provisions. For UK residents, this typically means referencing the Defamation Act 2013, UK GDPR, or the Data Protection Act 2018.
- **Exact quoted text from each URL**: you must quote the specific passages that are defamatory or otherwise unlawful. Do not paraphrase. Copy the exact text.

### Additional Uses

The Google legal removal route also covers:
- **Google Reviews**: defamatory or fake reviews on Google Business Profiles can be reported through this system
- **Copyright issues**: content that infringes your copyright can be reported under DMCA (Digital Millennium Copyright Act) provisions
- **Court orders**: if you have obtained a court order requiring removal, this can be submitted directly

## Submission Tips for Maximum Effectiveness

The quality of your submission significantly affects the likelihood of success. Google reviews thousands of requests and applies a structured assessment process. Follow these guidelines:

### 1. Argue That the URL, Title, and Snippet Are Defamatory

Google's search results display three elements for each result: the URL, the page title, and a text snippet. Your submission should argue that the combination of these elements is defamatory, even without clicking through to the full page. This is important because Google may be more inclined to act if the defamatory impression is created directly within the search results themselves.

### 2. Quote All Defamatory Content

Do not summarise or paraphrase. Copy and paste every defamatory statement from the page, and for each one, explain specifically why it is defamatory. Common categories include:
- Statements that imply criminal behaviour
- Statements that damage professional reputation
- Statements that are factually false and verifiable as such
- Statements that constitute harassment or invasion of privacy

### 3. State the Relevant Law

Vague claims of unfairness are insufficient. You must cite the specific legal framework that makes the content unlawful. For UK-based requests:
- **Defamation Act 2013**: the content must constitute a statement of fact (not opinion) that causes or is likely to cause serious harm to reputation
- **UK GDPR Article 17**: the right to erasure where personal data is no longer necessary, was processed unlawfully, or where the data subject withdraws consent
- **Data Protection Act 2018**: supplementary provisions to UK GDPR

### 4. One URL Per Argument

If you are submitting multiple URLs, provide a separate, tailored argument for each one. Do not submit a batch of URLs with a single generic explanation. Google assesses each URL individually.

### 5. Provide Supporting Evidence

Where possible, attach or reference supporting documentation: court documents, official records, evidence of corrections by the publisher, proof that allegations were unfounded, or documentation showing that circumstances have changed since the content was published.

### 6. Follow Up

Google does not guarantee response times. If you have not received a response within 30 days, submit a follow-up through the same form referencing your original submission. Keep records of all submission confirmations and reference numbers.

## What Happens After Submission

Google will review your request and make a determination. Possible outcomes include:

- **URL de-indexed**: the URL is removed from search results for the relevant country-specific Google domains. You will receive confirmation by email.
- **Request denied**: Google determines that the content does not meet the threshold for removal. You will receive a brief explanation. You can resubmit with additional evidence or arguments.
- **Partial action**: Google may de-index some URLs but not others, or may de-index for some search queries but not all.

De-indexing is not permanent by default. Google may periodically re-evaluate, particularly if the source content changes. Monitor your search results regularly and be prepared to resubmit if de-indexed URLs reappear.

## Combining De-indexing with Other Strategies

Google legal de-indexing is most effective when combined with other approaches:

- **Source removal**: if you can get the content removed from the original website, the de-indexing becomes permanent because there is nothing left to index.
- **Content suppression**: building positive content that ranks above any remaining negative results.
- **Right to be Forgotten requests**: a separate but related process under GDPR that can be pursued in parallel.

Google legal de-indexing is a powerful tool, but it requires careful preparation, specific legal arguments, and patience. A well-crafted submission that demonstrates clear legal grounds and provides comprehensive evidence has the best chance of success.
