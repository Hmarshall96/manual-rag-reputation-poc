# How to Request Criminal Record Search Deletion

Criminal records in the UK are held across multiple databases and can appear in background checks, DBS (Disclosure and Barring Service) certificates, and online searches for years or even decades after the original incident. For individuals whose records relate to false allegations, cases where no crime was committed, or minor matters that are disproportionately affecting their lives, there are formal processes for requesting deletion. This guide covers the databases involved, the eligibility criteria, and the step-by-step application process.

## Understanding the UK Criminal Records Databases

Criminal record information in the UK is held primarily in two databases:

### PNC -- Police National Computer

The PNC is the main criminal records database used by UK law enforcement. It holds records of:

- Convictions
- Cautions (simple cautions, conditional cautions, youth cautions)
- Arrests
- Reprimands and warnings
- Penalty notices for disorder

PNC records are retained until the individual's 100th birthday by default. This means that even minor matters can remain on the system for the rest of your life unless active steps are taken to request deletion.

### PND -- Police National Database

The PND holds "soft" intelligence -- information that has not resulted in a formal criminal justice outcome. This includes:

- Intelligence reports
- Domestic incident records
- Stop and search records
- Witness and victim information
- Custody records

PND records are retained for a minimum of 6 years, though retention periods vary depending on the type of information and the policies of the individual police force that entered the data.

## Eligibility for Deletion

Not all records can be deleted. ACRO (the Criminal Records Office) manages the deletion process and has established clear criteria for which applications will be considered.

### Eligible Scenarios

You may apply for deletion if:

- **No crime was committed**: the incident that led to the record was not actually a criminal offence. For example, a civil dispute that was incorrectly recorded as a crime.
- **Malicious or false allegations**: you were the subject of allegations that were demonstrably false or made maliciously. You must provide corroborative evidence to support this claim, such as witness statements, communications from the complainant retracting the allegation, or police/CPS records confirming the allegation was unfounded.
- **Proven alibi**: you can demonstrate that you were not present at the time or place of the alleged offence, supported by evidence such as travel records, CCTV, or independent witness statements.
- **Suspect status unclear at arrest**: you were arrested but it was subsequently established that you should not have been treated as a suspect.

### Not Eligible

You cannot apply for deletion if:

- **A court conviction exists**: records arising from court proceedings resulting in a conviction cannot be deleted through the ACRO process. These are subject to the Rehabilitation of Offenders Act 1974 (see below).
- **Investigation or proceedings are ongoing**: if the matter is still under investigation or court proceedings have not concluded, the record cannot be deleted until the process is complete.

## The ACRO Record Deletion Process

### Step 1: Contact ACRO

The initial point of contact for criminal record deletion requests is:

**Email**: deletions@acro.police.uk

Send an email expressing your wish to apply for deletion of your PNC record. ACRO will respond with the application form and guidance notes.

### Step 2: Complete the Application Form

The application form requires the following information:

#### Personal Details
- Full name (including any former names)
- Date of birth
- Address history

#### Proof of Identity
You must provide copies of:
- A valid passport or driving licence (photo identification)
- Proof of current address (utility bill, bank statement, council tax bill dated within the last three months)

#### Event Details
For each record you want deleted, provide:
- **Police force**: the force that handled the matter
- **Police station**: the station where you were processed
- **Date of the event**: as precise as possible
- **Offence**: the offence you were arrested for or charged with
- **Outcome**: what happened (no further action, charges dropped, acquittal, etc.)

#### Supporting Evidence
Provide any documentation that supports your case for deletion. This might include:
- Court documents showing acquittal or dismissal
- Police correspondence confirming no further action
- Evidence that allegations were false or malicious
- Alibi evidence
- Character references (less impactful but can supplement stronger evidence)

### Step 3: ACRO Review

ACRO will review your application and, if it meets the eligibility criteria, forward it to the relevant chief constable for a decision. This is an important point: ACRO facilitates the process, but the decision to delete rests with the chief constable of the police force that "owns" the data.

### Step 4: Chief Constable's Decision

Chief constables exercise discretion when deciding whether to delete PNC records. Key points about this decision:

- Chief constables "own" PNC data for records created by their force
- For non-court disposals (cautions, arrests without charge), chief constables have broad discretion
- The NPCC (National Police Chiefs' Council) provides guidance, but there is no fixed criterion -- professional judgement applies
- Factors considered include: the nature of the offence, the outcome, the time elapsed, the impact on the individual, and any ongoing risk assessment
- The decision is not subject to a formal appeals process, though you can request a review or escalate through the police complaints procedure

### Step 5: Notification

You will be notified of the decision by ACRO. If the deletion is approved, the record will be removed from the PNC. If refused, you will receive an explanation of the reasons.

## Rehabilitation of Offenders Act 1974

For court convictions that cannot be deleted through the ACRO process, the Rehabilitation of Offenders Act 1974 provides a framework for when convictions become "spent." Once a conviction is spent:

- It does not need to be disclosed in most circumstances (job applications, insurance, etc.)
- It should not appear on standard DBS checks
- It may still appear on enhanced DBS checks for certain sensitive roles

Rehabilitation periods depend on the sentence imposed:

- **Conditional discharge**: spent after the period of the order
- **Fine**: spent after 1 year (adults)
- **Community order**: spent after 1 year after the order ends
- **Custody up to 6 months**: spent after 2 years
- **Custody 6-30 months**: spent after 4 years
- **Custody 30-48 months**: spent after 7 years
- **Custody over 48 months**: never spent

These periods were significantly reduced by the Legal Aid, Sentencing and Punishment of Offenders Act 2012 for offences committed after its commencement.

## Connection to Right to Be Forgotten

A spent conviction under the Rehabilitation of Offenders Act significantly strengthens a Right to Be Forgotten (RTBF) request to Google or other search engines. When arguing for de-indexing of content related to a criminal record:

- A spent conviction demonstrates that the legal system considers the individual to have been rehabilitated
- The continued visibility of the conviction in search results is disproportionate to the public interest
- UK GDPR Article 17 provides the legal basis, and the spent status of the conviction is a powerful supporting argument

Combining criminal record deletion (where eligible) with RTBF requests and content suppression provides the most comprehensive approach to managing the online impact of a criminal record.

## Practical Tips

- **Start early**: the ACRO process can take several months from initial application to final decision. Begin as soon as you are eligible.
- **Be thorough**: incomplete applications are the most common cause of delays. Ensure all required information and evidence is provided with your initial submission.
- **Gather evidence proactively**: if your case involves false allegations, collect and preserve evidence as early as possible. Witness recollections fade and documents become harder to obtain over time.
- **Consider legal advice**: for complex cases, particularly those involving multiple forces or contested facts, specialist legal advice can improve the quality of your application.
- **Address all databases**: deletion from the PNC does not automatically mean deletion from the PND or from individual police force systems. Ask ACRO specifically about PND records and any local force databases.
- **Monitor DBS checks**: after successful deletion, verify that the record no longer appears by requesting a basic DBS check on yourself through the DBS website.

Criminal record deletion is a detailed administrative process, but for eligible individuals it provides a clear path to removing records that should never have existed or that are no longer proportionate. The key is establishing clear eligibility and providing compelling evidence with your application.
