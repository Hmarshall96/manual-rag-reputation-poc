# How to Appeal a Google RTBF Rejection

## Overview

A rejected Right to Be Forgotten (RTBF) request is not the end of the road. Google's initial rejection often reflects a specific objection that can be addressed through a revised submission with additional evidence, adjusted framing, or a different submission strategy. This guide covers how to interpret rejection reasons, develop an appeal strategy, and resubmit with the strongest possible case.

## Understanding Google's Rejection Language

Google uses specific language in its rejection emails. Learning to decode this language is essential for crafting a successful appeal. Below are the most common rejection phrases and what they actually mean.

### "Relates to your current job or profession"

Google has determined that the content is relevant to your professional life and therefore has a public interest justification. This is one of the most common rejection reasons.

**What to do**: Provide evidence that the content does not relate to your current role. If you have changed profession, supply documentation (new employment contract, LinkedIn profile, Companies House records). If the content relates to a previous business that has closed, provide dissolution records.

### "Substantial interest to the public regarding your professional life"

Similar to the above, but broader. Google believes the public has a legitimate interest in the information because of your professional activities.

**What to do**: Argue that the public interest has diminished over time. Provide evidence of changed circumstances. If the content relates to a one-off incident rather than a pattern, emphasise this.

### "Requesting documents for criminal charges"

Google wants to see official documentation about the status of criminal proceedings -- whether charges were dropped, a conviction is spent, or a sentence has been completed.

**What to do**: Obtain and provide official documentation. This might include a basic DBS check showing no current convictions, court records showing charges were dropped or dismissed, or a letter from a solicitor confirming the conviction is spent under the Rehabilitation of Offenders Act 1974.

### "We've decided not to delist this content... considered factors like how relevant the content is to the public"

This is a general rejection indicating Google weighed the public interest against privacy rights and came down on the side of public interest.

**What to do**: You need to shift the balance. Provide new evidence, wait for more time to pass (making the content older and less relevant), or reframe your arguments to address the specific public interest concern.

## Case Study: Vadim Shulman -- When Timing Works Against You

**Situation**: A request was submitted to delist a news article dated 18 November 2024. The article came from a news site and covered a matter of public interest.

**Google's response**: "We've decided not to delist this content... considered factors like how relevant the content is to the public."

**Analysis**: This rejection was almost inevitable. The content was extremely recent, originated from a news source (which Google gives strong deference to), and related to a matter of public interest. All three factors worked against delisting.

**Lesson**: Timing matters enormously. Very recent news content is almost impossible to delist. In cases like this, the best strategy is to wait 12-24 months and resubmit when the content has aged and lost its immediate newsworthiness. In the interim, focus on suppression strategies (pushing the content down in search results through positive content creation) rather than removal.

## Case Study: Owen Saunders -- The Power of Resubmission Strategy

**Situation**: Owen Saunders had a criminal record from 2014 related to cowboy builder activities. The first RTBF request was submitted by an agent using a professional email address (legal@igniyte.com).

**First attempt result**: Rejected. Google stated the information "relates to your current job or profession" and involved professional conduct.

**What changed for the second attempt**:

1. **Submitted through a personal email address** rather than an agent's professional email. This made the request appear to come directly from the data subject rather than a reputation management firm.
2. **Additional time had passed**, making the content older and less relevant.
3. **The framing was adjusted** to emphasise that the conviction was spent and the individual's circumstances had changed.

**Second attempt result**: Successful. The URLs were delisted.

**Key lessons**:
- Submitting via a personal email rather than an agent email can make a significant difference. Google may apply greater scrutiny to requests that clearly come from reputation management companies.
- A rejection on the first attempt does not preclude success on a subsequent attempt, particularly if circumstances or strategy change.
- The passage of time between attempts works in your favour.

## Step-by-Step Appeal Process

### Step 1: Analyse the Rejection

Read Google's rejection email carefully. Identify the specific reason or reasons given. Map each reason to the categories described above. This tells you exactly what you need to address.

### Step 2: Gather Additional Evidence

Based on the rejection reason, collect documentation that directly counters Google's objection:

| Rejection Reason | Evidence to Gather |
|---|---|
| Relates to current profession | New employment records, Companies House filings showing directorship ended, evidence of career change |
| Public interest in professional life | Evidence that the matter is resolved, business closed, role changed |
| Criminal charges | DBS check, court records, solicitor's letter confirming spent conviction, proof charges were dropped |
| Content too recent | Wait 12-24 months, then document how circumstances have changed in the interim |
| Content from news source | Evidence of factual inaccuracies, evidence the article is misleading, ICO complaints |

### Step 3: Decide on Submission Strategy

Consider the following tactical decisions:

**Who submits the request?**
- If the first request was submitted by an agent, consider resubmitting through the data subject's personal email address.
- If the first request was submitted by the data subject, consider whether a solicitor's letter accompanying the resubmission would add weight.

**When to resubmit?**
- If the rejection was based on recency, wait at least 6-12 months.
- If the rejection was based on insufficient evidence, resubmit as soon as you have the evidence.
- If the rejection was based on professional relevance and circumstances have genuinely changed, resubmit with proof of the change.

**How to frame the resubmission?**
- Do not simply repeat the same arguments. Acknowledge (implicitly) the previous objection and address it directly.
- Lead with your strongest new evidence.
- If a conviction is now spent when it was not before, this is a powerful new fact to lead with.

### Step 4: Rewrite the Per-URL Explanations

Draft entirely new explanations for each URL. Do not copy and paste from the original submission. Address the rejection reason head-on in the first sentence, then build your case with new evidence.

**Example resubmission language for a "relates to profession" rejection:**

> "I no longer work in the field described in this article. I left [industry/company] in [year] and now work in [different field]. I enclose [Companies House records / employment documentation] confirming this change. The content is no longer relevant to my current professional activities and serves no public interest purpose. The conviction referenced is spent under the Rehabilitation of Offenders Act 1974."

### Step 5: Submit and Document

Resubmit through the same RTBF form at https://reportcontent.google.com/forms/rtbf. Keep a complete record of:

- The date of resubmission
- The email address used
- The exact text of each per-URL explanation
- All supporting documents submitted
- Any follow-up correspondence from Google

### Step 6: Respond to Follow-Ups Promptly

If Google sends follow-up questions after a resubmission, treat this as a positive signal. Respond within 24-48 hours with thorough, evidence-backed answers.

## Escalation Beyond Google

If repeated resubmissions fail, there are further options:

1. **ICO complaint**: In the UK, you can complain to the Information Commissioner's Office that Google has failed to comply with data protection law. The ICO can investigate and order Google to delist.
2. **Data Protection Authority complaint**: In EU member states, the equivalent national data protection authority can be approached.
3. **Court order**: As a last resort, a court order requiring delisting can be obtained. This is expensive but definitive. Google complies with court orders.
4. **Suppression strategy**: If removal proves impossible, pivot to suppression -- creating and promoting positive content to push the offending URLs off page one of search results. This does not remove the content but eliminates its practical impact for the vast majority of searchers.

## Timeline Expectations

- **Resubmission processing**: Approximately 14 days, same as initial submissions.
- **ICO complaint**: 3-6 months for investigation and decision.
- **Court proceedings**: 6-18 months depending on complexity and jurisdiction.
- **Suppression campaign**: 3-6 months to see significant movement in search results.

## Summary

RTBF rejections are common but not final. The key to a successful appeal is understanding why Google rejected the initial request, gathering evidence that directly addresses the objection, and making strategic choices about who submits, when, and how the arguments are framed. The Owen Saunders case demonstrates that even straightforward changes -- such as switching from an agent email to a personal email -- can turn a rejection into a success. Patience, preparation, and persistence are the three pillars of effective RTBF appeals.
