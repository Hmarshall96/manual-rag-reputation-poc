# How to Submit a Google Right to Be Forgotten (RTBF) Request

## Overview

The Right to Be Forgotten (RTBF) allows individuals in the EU and UK to request that Google delist search results that are inadequate, irrelevant, no longer relevant, or excessive in relation to the purposes for which they were processed. This guide walks through the entire process of preparing and submitting a successful RTBF request, including the arguments that carry the most weight and the evidence you should gather before filing.

## Prerequisites

Before you begin, make sure you have the following ready:

- The full legal name of the data subject (the person whose information appears in search results)
- The name as it appears when searched on Google (if different from the legal name)
- The country of residence of the data subject
- A contact email address
- A complete list of URLs you want delisted, with a clear explanation for each (up to 1,000 characters per URL)
- Any supporting documentation that strengthens your case (court records, company filings, evidence that a conviction is spent, etc.)

## Step 1: Access the RTBF Form

Navigate to Google's official content removal request form at:

**https://reportcontent.google.com/forms/rtbf**

This is the only legitimate channel for submitting RTBF requests to Google. Do not use generic contact forms or email addresses.

## Step 2: Complete the Required Fields

The form will ask for the following information:

1. **Country**: Select the country where the data subject resides. This determines which data protection regime applies.
2. **Name used to search**: Enter the exact name (or name variation) that produces the offending search results. This may differ from the legal name.
3. **Full legal name**: Provide the data subject's full legal name as it appears on official documents.
4. **Relationship to the data subject**: If you are submitting on behalf of someone else (as an agent or representative), you must state your relationship and confirm you have authority to act.
5. **Contact email**: Provide a working email address. Google will use this for all correspondence, including follow-up questions and the final decision.
6. **URLs to remove**: List every URL you want delisted. Be thorough -- each URL must be submitted individually with its own explanation.
7. **Explanation per URL**: You have a 1,000-character limit per URL. Use this space to make a concise, compelling argument for delisting.

## Step 3: Craft Your Arguments

The strength of your request depends almost entirely on the quality of your per-URL explanations. Google evaluates requests against specific criteria, so your language should map directly to those criteria.

### Core Arguments to Use

Frame your explanation around one or more of the following grounds:

- **Irrelevant**: The information has no bearing on the data subject's current life or activities.
- **No longer relevant**: The information was once relevant but circumstances have changed (e.g., a spent conviction, a closed business, a resolved legal matter).
- **Inadequate**: The information gives an incomplete or misleading picture.
- **Excessive**: The information goes beyond what is necessary for any legitimate purpose.
- **Outdated**: The information is old and no longer reflects reality.
- **Invasive of privacy**: The information intrudes unreasonably on the data subject's private life.

### Template Language

Use language along these lines in your explanation:

> "Further to the ECJ ruling, I object to the links in question remaining accessible to Google users. The information to which it links is irrelevant/no longer relevant/inadequate/excessive. It is out of date and can be of no conceivable public interest. The content is invasive of my reasonable expectations regarding privacy."

Tailor this template to each specific URL. Generic copy-paste explanations are less effective than URL-specific reasoning.

## Step 4: Anticipate Google's Evaluation Criteria

Google weighs several factors when reviewing RTBF requests. Understanding these helps you pre-empt objections:

- **Your role in public life**: Public figures (politicians, executives, public-facing professionals) face a higher bar. If the data subject is a private individual, emphasise this.
- **Source of information**: Google defers strongly to government publications and journalist editorial judgments. Content from official government sources or established news outlets is harder to delist.
- **Age of content**: Older content is easier to delist. If the content is several years old, state the date explicitly and argue it has lost relevance over time.
- **Criminal record**: If the content relates to a criminal conviction, provide evidence that the sentence is spent under the Rehabilitation of Offenders Act 1974 (or equivalent legislation). Spent convictions carry significant weight.
- **Factual accuracy**: If the content is factually inaccurate or misleading, highlight the specific inaccuracies.

## Step 5: Submit and Wait

After submission, Google typically processes RTBF requests within approximately 14 days, though this can vary. You will receive an email confirmation and may receive follow-up questions.

**Important**: If Google asks follow-up questions, respond promptly and thoroughly. Follow-up requests are a positive sign -- they indicate Google is actively considering your case rather than rejecting it outright.

## Success Rates by Content Type

Understanding the baseline acceptance rates helps set realistic expectations:

| Content Type | Approximate Success Rate |
|---|---|
| Online directories | 54.6% |
| Blog posts | 26.8% |
| Social networks | 26.7% |
| Press/news sites | 19.0% |
| Wikipedia | 10.6% |
| **Overall average** | **~29%** |

These figures show that directory listings are the most likely to be delisted, while news sites and Wikipedia are significantly harder targets. Adjust your strategy and client expectations accordingly.

## Real-World Examples

### Example 1: Ian Fenny -- Spent Environmental Conviction

**Situation**: A 17-year-old environmental conviction was appearing in Google search results. The individual was no longer a director of the company involved.

**Approach**: The RTBF request argued the conviction was no longer relevant to Mr Fenny's current occupation. Supporting evidence included Companies House records confirming he was no longer a director of the relevant company.

**Outcome**: Google asked a follow-up question about Mr Fenny's current employment. Additional supporting evidence was provided demonstrating his current role had no connection to the conviction. Google delisted the URLs.

**Key takeaway**: Be prepared for follow-up questions. Having supporting documentation ready to send quickly can make the difference between success and failure.

### Example 2: Reuben Barney-Smith -- Misleading Article Title

**Situation**: A news article used a headline that lumped together a minor planning dispute with an unrelated animal neglect case, creating a misleading impression.

**Approach**: The RTBF request argued the content was misleading and inaccurate, citing ICO criteria. The argument focused on the inadequacy of the information rather than its age.

**Outcome**: Google delisted the article from search results for the individual's name.

**Key takeaway**: The "inadequate" and "misleading" arguments can succeed even when content is relatively recent, if you can demonstrate that the information presents a genuinely distorted picture.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Submitting without evidence**: Assertions without supporting documentation are weak. Always attach or reference evidence.
2. **Using generic explanations**: Identical explanations for multiple URLs signal a lazy request. Tailor each explanation.
3. **Ignoring the public interest test**: If there is a plausible public interest argument, address it head-on rather than hoping Google will not raise it.
4. **Submitting too many URLs at once**: Large batches can trigger closer scrutiny. Consider submitting in focused groups of related URLs.
5. **Failing to respond to follow-ups**: Silence after a follow-up question is treated as abandonment of the request.

## Next Steps After Submission

- Monitor the listed URLs in Google search results for the relevant name queries.
- If the request is successful, verify delisting across relevant Google country domains (.co.uk, .de, .fr, etc.).
- If the request is rejected, see **H02: RTBF Rejection Appeal** for the appeal process and strategies for resubmission.
- Keep records of all correspondence with Google for future reference.

## Summary

A successful RTBF request requires careful preparation, strong per-URL arguments mapped to Google's evaluation criteria, and supporting evidence. The overall acceptance rate of approximately 29% means rejection is common, but well-prepared requests with strong arguments -- particularly for older content, spent convictions, or misleading information -- have a significantly higher chance of success.
