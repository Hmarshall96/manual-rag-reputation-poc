# How to File a DMCA Copyright Removal Notice

## Overview

The Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA) provides a powerful mechanism for removing copyrighted content that has been published online without authorisation. Under 17 U.S.C. 512(c)(3), copyright holders can send a formal takedown notice to the hosting provider or platform, which must then remove the infringing material to maintain its safe harbour protection. This guide covers when to use a DMCA notice, the required elements, how to submit one, and important limitations to understand.

## When to Use a DMCA Notice

DMCA takedown notices are appropriate when:

- **Photographs or images** belonging to you or your client have been republished without permission
- **Written content** (articles, blog posts, reports) has been copied and republished on another site
- **Videos** have been uploaded to a platform without the copyright holder's consent
- **Documents** (PDFs, presentations, reports) have been shared without authorisation
- **Any original creative work** protected by copyright has been reproduced without a licence

DMCA notices are particularly useful in reputation management for removing **unauthorised images and photographs** from websites. When a site has used someone's photo without permission -- for example, alongside defamatory or negative content -- a DMCA notice targeting the image can sometimes result in the removal of the entire page or a significant reduction in its search visibility.

**Important limitation**: A DMCA notice can only be used for content you own the copyright to. You cannot use it to remove content written by someone else about you, even if that content is negative or defamatory. For that, use defamation takedown letters (see H03) or RTBF requests (see H01).

## Required Elements Under 17 U.S.C. 512(c)(3)

A valid DMCA takedown notice must contain all of the following elements. An incomplete notice may be rejected or ignored.

### 1. Identification of the Copyrighted Work

Clearly identify the copyrighted work that has been infringed. Provide:

- A description of the original work
- Where the original work was first published (URL, publication name, date)
- Evidence of ownership (registration number if available, or evidence of creation such as metadata, original files, publication dates)

If multiple copyrighted works on a single site have been infringed, you may provide a representative list.

### 2. Identification of the Infringing Material

Identify the material that infringes your copyright with enough specificity that the hosting provider can locate it:

- The exact URL(s) where the infringing material appears
- A description of the infringing material on each page
- If the infringing material is part of a larger page (e.g., an image embedded in an article), identify the specific element

### 3. Contact Information

Provide sufficient contact information for the hosting provider to reach you:

- Full legal name
- Mailing address
- Telephone number
- Email address

### 4. Good Faith Belief Statement

Include the following statement (or substantially similar language):

> "I have a good faith belief that the use of the copyrighted materials described above on the infringing web pages is not authorised by the copyright owner, or its agent, or the law."

This is a required element. Do not omit it.

### 5. Accuracy Statement Under Penalty of Perjury

Include the following statement:

> "I swear, under penalty of perjury, that the information in this notification is accurate and that I am the copyright owner, or am authorised to act on behalf of the owner, of an exclusive right that is allegedly infringed."

**Warning**: This statement is made under penalty of perjury. Filing a false DMCA notice can result in legal liability. Only file a DMCA notice if you genuinely own the copyright to the material in question.

### 6. Physical or Electronic Signature

The notice must be signed. An electronic signature (typed name with "/s/" prefix, or a scanned signature) is acceptable.

## Template DMCA Takedown Notice

The following template incorporates all required elements. Adapt it to your specific circumstances.

---

**DMCA Takedown Notice**

Date: [Date]

To: [Hosting Provider / Website Operator / DMCA Designated Agent]

Re: Copyright Infringement -- Notice under 17 U.S.C. 512(c)(3)

Dear Sir/Madam,

The following information serves to assert my rights and request removal of allegedly infringing web content under the Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA).

**1. Copyrighted Work**

I am the copyright owner of [description of original work]. This work was first published at [URL / publication / date]. [If registered: The work is registered with the U.S. Copyright Office under registration number [number].]

**2. Infringing Material**

The following URL(s) contain material that infringes my copyright:

- [URL 1] -- Contains [description of infringing material, e.g., "my photograph originally published at (original URL)"]
- [URL 2] -- Contains [description]

[Add additional URLs as needed]

**3. Contact Information**

Name: [Full legal name]
Address: [Mailing address]
Telephone: [Phone number]
Email: [Email address]

**4. Good Faith Statement**

I have a good faith belief that the use of the copyrighted materials described above on the infringing web pages is not authorised by the copyright owner, or its agent, or the law.

**5. Accuracy Statement**

I swear, under penalty of perjury, that the information in this notification is accurate and that I am the copyright owner, or am authorised to act on behalf of the owner, of an exclusive right that is allegedly infringed.

**6. Signature**

/s/ [Full legal name]

---

## Where to Send the Notice

### To the Website's Hosting Provider

Most websites are hosted by third-party hosting providers (e.g., GoDaddy, Cloudflare, AWS, OVH). The hosting provider is legally obligated to act on valid DMCA notices.

To find the hosting provider:
1. Use a WHOIS lookup tool (e.g., whois.domaintools.com) to find the domain's hosting information
2. Check the hosting provider's website for their designated DMCA agent (usually found in their Terms of Service or Legal pages)
3. Send the notice to the designated DMCA agent

### To the Website Operator Directly

If you can identify the website operator's contact information, send the notice directly to them as well. Many websites publish a "Contact" or "Legal" page with relevant email addresses.

### To Google

If you want the infringing content removed from Google search results (in addition to or instead of removal from the source website), you can file a DMCA notice directly with Google:

**Google Legal Removal Request**: https://support.google.com/legal/contact/lr_legalother

Google will review the notice and, if valid, remove the infringing URLs from its search index. This does not remove the content from the source website, but it prevents it from appearing in Google search results.

Filing with Google is particularly useful when:
- The source website is unresponsive
- The hosting provider is in a jurisdiction that does not recognise DMCA
- You want to quickly reduce the visibility of the infringing content while pursuing removal from the source

## Step-by-Step Process

### Step 1: Document the Infringement

Before sending any notice:
- Screenshot the infringing content with timestamps
- Save the page using the Wayback Machine (web.archive.org) or a similar archival service
- Document the original work with proof of ownership (original files, metadata, publication dates)

### Step 2: Identify the Recipient

Determine who to send the notice to:
- The website operator (check the website's Contact/Legal pages)
- The hosting provider's designated DMCA agent (check WHOIS and the host's legal pages)
- Google (if you want search delisting)

### Step 3: Prepare and Send the Notice

Use the template above, filling in all required fields. Send via email to the designated DMCA agent. Keep a copy of everything you send.

### Step 4: Wait for Response

Hosting providers are expected to act "expeditiously" upon receiving a valid DMCA notice. In practice:
- Most reputable hosting providers respond within 24-72 hours
- Google typically processes DMCA notices within 1-2 weeks
- Some providers may forward the notice to the website operator and give them a period to respond before taking action

### Step 5: Follow Up

If no action is taken within a reasonable timeframe (7-10 days for hosting providers, 2-3 weeks for Google):
- Resend the notice with a follow-up message
- If the hosting provider is unresponsive, file with Google to at least remove the content from search results
- Consider whether legal proceedings are warranted

## Important Limitations

1. **Filing a DMCA notice does not guarantee removal of every instance.** The notice only applies to the specific URLs listed. If the content has been copied to multiple sites, you must send separate notices for each.

2. **Counter-notices**: The alleged infringer has the right to file a counter-notice disputing your claim. If they do, the hosting provider may restore the content after 10-14 business days unless you file a court action.

3. **Abuse of DMCA**: Filing false DMCA notices is illegal and can result in liability for damages. Only file notices for content you genuinely hold copyright over.

4. **Jurisdiction**: DMCA is a US law. It is most effective against US-based hosting providers and platforms. However, many international hosting providers voluntarily comply with DMCA procedures, and Google applies DMCA globally.

5. **Fair use**: Content that uses your copyrighted material under fair use (criticism, commentary, news reporting, education) may not be infringing. Consider whether a fair use defence might apply before filing.

## Summary

DMCA takedown notices are an effective tool for removing copyrighted content that has been published without authorisation. They are particularly useful in reputation management for removing unauthorised photographs and images. A valid notice must include all six required elements under 17 U.S.C. 512(c)(3), must be sent to the correct designated agent, and must be filed in good faith. For maximum effectiveness, file with both the hosting provider and Google simultaneously. Understand the limitations: DMCA covers copyright infringement only, not defamation or other harms, and each instance of infringement requires its own notice.
