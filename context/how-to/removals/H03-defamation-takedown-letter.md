# How to Send a Defamation Takedown Letter

## Overview

A defamation takedown letter is a formal legal notice sent to the host or publisher of defamatory online content, demanding its removal. When constructed properly and sent through the right channels, these letters are highly effective at securing removal of defamatory material from websites, social media platforms, and web hosts. This guide covers the legal basis, structure, delivery, and practical tips for maximising the success rate of defamation takedown letters.

## Legal Basis

Defamation takedown letters draw on two primary legal frameworks:

### UK Defamation Act 2013

The Defamation Act 2013 governs defamation claims in England and Wales. Key provisions relevant to takedown letters:

- **Section 1**: A statement is not defamatory unless its publication has caused or is likely to cause serious harm to the reputation of the claimant. For businesses, this means serious financial loss.
- **Section 5**: Provides a defence for website operators who did not post the defamatory statement, but only if they comply with the regulations for handling complaints (which includes responding to takedown notices).
- **Section 10**: A court cannot hear an action against someone who is not the primary publisher (e.g., a web host) unless it is not reasonably practicable to bring an action against the primary publisher.

### E-Commerce Directive 2000/31/EC -- Article 14

Article 14 provides a "safe harbour" for hosting providers, exempting them from liability for hosted content **unless** they have been notified of unlawful content and fail to act. This is the critical mechanism that makes takedown letters effective against web hosts and platforms.

Once a hosting provider receives actual notice of defamatory content (through a properly constructed takedown letter), they lose their Article 14 protection if they do not act expeditiously to remove the content. This creates a strong commercial incentive for hosts to comply.

## Who Should Send the Letter

The letter must be sent on **solicitor's headed notepaper**. This is not optional. A takedown letter from an individual or a non-legal representative carries significantly less weight. The solicitor's involvement signals:

- The claim has been assessed by a legal professional and has merit
- The claimant is prepared to take legal action
- Ignoring the letter carries real legal and financial risk

If you do not have a solicitor, engaging one for the purpose of sending a takedown letter is a worthwhile investment. Many solicitors will draft and send a takedown letter for a fixed fee.

## Who to Send It To

Identify the correct recipient. The letter should be addressed to:

1. **The legal department** of the website, platform, or hosting provider
2. **The directors** of the company that operates the website or hosting service
3. **The designated agent** for legal complaints (many platforms publish this on their website)

For websites hosted by third-party providers, you may need to send the letter to the web host rather than (or in addition to) the website operator. Use WHOIS lookup tools to identify the hosting provider if it is not obvious.

## Letter Structure

A properly constructed defamation takedown letter follows a specific structure. Each element serves a purpose and should not be omitted.

### 1. Header and Title

The letter should be clearly titled:

> **Notice and Take-Down Letter under Article 14 of the E-Commerce Directive (2000/31/EC)**

This immediately signals the legal basis and puts the recipient on notice that their safe harbour protection is at stake.

### 2. Identification of the Client

State clearly who you represent:

> "We act on behalf of [Client Name] in relation to defamatory content published on [website/platform name] at the following URL(s)."

### 3. Identification of the Offending Content

Quote the specific defamatory statements. Do not simply link to the page -- extract and quote the exact words that are defamatory. Identify each statement with:

- The exact URL where it appears
- The date it was published (if known)
- The exact words complained of (quoted verbatim)
- Who posted it (if known; if anonymous, state this)

### 4. Explanation of Why the Content Is Defamatory

For each statement identified, explain clearly and concisely why it is defamatory. A statement is defamatory if it:

- Tends to lower the claimant in the estimation of right-thinking members of society
- Causes or is likely to cause serious harm to the claimant's reputation
- Is a statement of fact (not opinion) that is false, or a statement of opinion not based on true facts

Be specific. Do not simply assert "this is defamatory." Explain the meaning conveyed and why it is false and damaging.

**Example:**

> "The statement '[quoted text]' published at [URL] is factually false. Our client has never been [subject of the false claim]. This statement tends to lower our client in the estimation of right-thinking members of society and has caused/is likely to cause serious harm to our client's reputation, including [specific harm if possible]."

### 5. Formal Notice Under Article 14(1)(a)

This is the critical legal mechanism. State explicitly:

> "This letter constitutes formal notice under Article 14(1)(a) of the E-Commerce Directive (2000/31/EC). You are hereby put on notice of the unlawful nature of the content identified above. From receipt of this letter, you can no longer claim the hosting defence under Article 14, and you will be liable for the continued publication of this defamatory content."

### 6. Demand for Removal

State your demand clearly and set a deadline:

> "We require that you remove the content identified above within 48 hours of receipt of this letter."

The 48-hour timeframe is standard. It is short enough to convey urgency but long enough to be considered reasonable.

### 7. Consequences of Non-Compliance

State the consequences clearly:

> "If the content is not removed within the timeframe specified, our client reserves the right to commence legal proceedings without further notice. Our client will seek:
> - An injunction requiring the immediate removal of the defamatory content
> - Damages for the harm caused to our client's reputation
> - Legal costs on an indemnity basis
> - Interest on any damages awarded"

## Template

The following template brings all elements together. Adapt it to the specific circumstances of each case.

---

**[Solicitor's Firm Name]**
**[Address]**
**[Date]**

**NOTICE AND TAKE-DOWN LETTER UNDER ARTICLE 14 OF THE E-COMMERCE DIRECTIVE (2000/31/EC)**

Dear Sir/Madam,

We act on behalf of [Client Name] ("our client") in relation to defamatory content hosted on your platform/website at the URL(s) listed below.

**Offending Content**

The following URL(s) contain statements that are defamatory of our client:

1. [URL] -- Published on [date]. The statement "[quoted defamatory text]" is factually false because [explanation]. This statement tends to lower our client in the estimation of right-thinking members of society and has caused serious harm to our client's reputation.

[Repeat for each URL/statement]

**Legal Basis**

The content identified above is defamatory within the meaning of the Defamation Act 2013. Publication of this content has caused, and continues to cause, serious harm to our client's reputation.

**Formal Notice**

This letter constitutes formal notice to you under Article 14(1)(a) of the E-Commerce Directive (2000/31/EC) of the unlawful nature of the above content. From receipt of this letter, you are deemed to have actual knowledge of the defamatory material hosted on your service and can no longer rely on the hosting defence provided by Article 14.

**Demand**

We require that you remove the identified content within 48 hours of receipt of this letter.

**Consequences**

If the content is not removed within 48 hours, our client will have no alternative but to commence proceedings seeking an injunction, damages, costs on an indemnity basis, and interest.

We trust this matter can be resolved without the need for litigation.

Yours faithfully,

[Solicitor Name]
[Firm Name]

---

## Successful Applications

Defamation takedown letters using this approach have been successfully deployed against:

- **Defamatory Facebook pages**: Where individuals or businesses have created pages specifically to attack someone's reputation
- **Defamatory websites**: Including purpose-built "gripe sites" and blogs
- **Web hosts**: Where the website operator is unresponsive, approaching the hosting provider directly with an Article 14 notice often results in the host removing the site or pressuring the operator to remove the content

## Practical Tips

1. **Send via tracked delivery**: Use email for speed, but also send a hard copy via recorded delivery. This creates an indisputable record of receipt.
2. **Keep copies of everything**: Screenshot the defamatory content before sending the letter. Websites can be modified or taken down, and you need evidence of what was published.
3. **Follow up**: If you receive no response within 48 hours, send a follow-up letter noting the deadline has passed and reiterating the intention to commence proceedings.
4. **Consider the publisher**: If the primary publisher (the person who wrote the defamatory content) is identifiable, consider sending a separate letter to them directly.
5. **Document the harm**: If possible, quantify the harm caused by the defamatory content (lost business, lost opportunities, emotional distress). This strengthens both the letter and any subsequent legal proceedings.
6. **Act promptly**: The longer defamatory content remains online, the more harm it causes and the harder it becomes to argue urgency. Send the takedown letter as soon as the defamatory content is identified.

## Limitations

- Takedown letters are not effective against content that is true. Truth is an absolute defence to defamation.
- Statements of honest opinion (as opposed to statements of fact) may be protected under Section 3 of the Defamation Act 2013.
- Some platforms and hosts are based in jurisdictions where UK and EU law has limited reach. In these cases, alternative strategies (such as DMCA notices for US-based hosts, or Google RTBF requests) may be more appropriate.

## Summary

A well-constructed defamation takedown letter, sent on solicitor's headed notepaper and invoking Article 14 of the E-Commerce Directive, is one of the most effective tools for removing defamatory online content. The key is precision: identify the exact defamatory statements, explain clearly why they are defamatory, give formal legal notice, demand removal within 48 hours, and outline the consequences of non-compliance. Most hosts and platforms will comply rather than risk losing their Article 14 safe harbour protection.
