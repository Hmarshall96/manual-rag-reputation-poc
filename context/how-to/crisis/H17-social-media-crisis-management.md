# How to Manage Social Media During a Crisis

Social media is the fastest channel for both reputation damage and reputation recovery. During a crisis, it is where the public conversation happens in real time and where your response will be judged most harshly. But as the Coral Sea Resorts case study demonstrated, it is also the most powerful tool for rebuilding a damaged reputation when used strategically over a sustained period.

## The Coral Sea Resorts Case Study: What the Numbers Show

The Coral Sea Resorts crisis recovery programme provides concrete evidence that social media, when managed with discipline and consistency, can drive extraordinary reputation recovery. Over the course of a 12-month sustained effort, the results were:

- **Facebook:** +352.5% growth in engagement and following.
- **Twitter:** +345% growth in engagement and following.
- **YouTube:** +95.9% growth in views and subscribers.
- **Instagram:** +383.3% growth in engagement and following.

These numbers did not happen by accident. They were the result of a structured, daily, content-driven strategy that prioritised volume, consistency, and genuine engagement. The lesson is clear: social media recovery is not a quick fix. It is a sustained campaign that, when executed properly, delivers results that exceed pre-crisis performance.

## Phase 1: Immediate Crisis Response on Social Media

When a crisis breaks, your social media channels require immediate attention. The first hours are critical, and every action (or inaction) will be scrutinised. Follow these steps in order.

### Pause All Scheduled Content

The first action is to pause or cancel all scheduled posts across every platform. Nothing damages credibility faster during a crisis than a cheerful promotional post appearing while the organisation is dealing with a serious incident. Review your content calendar and hold everything until the crisis management team gives clearance to resume.

### Activate Monitoring on All Channels

Begin monitoring all social media channels immediately. This includes your owned channels (Facebook, Twitter/X, Instagram, LinkedIn, YouTube, TikTok) as well as broader mentions across the web. You need to know:

- What is being said about the incident.
- Who is saying it (journalists, customers, influencers, anonymous accounts).
- How fast the conversation is spreading.
- What the dominant sentiment is.
- Whether any misinformation is circulating.

Use social listening tools if available, but do not wait for tools to be configured. Manual monitoring is better than no monitoring.

### Respond to Direct Inquiries with the Holding Statement Only

When people ask questions on your social media channels, respond only with the approved holding statement (see H16) or a shortened version appropriate for the platform. Do not engage in debate or allow individual team members to improvise responses. Keep responses consistent across all platforms.

### Do Not Delete Negative Comments

This is one of the most important rules of social media crisis management. Deleting negative comments creates the Streisand effect -- it draws more attention to the very thing you are trying to suppress. People screenshot deleted comments and share them widely, accompanied by accusations of censorship and cover-up.

Instead of deleting, respond professionally. Acknowledge the concern, direct them to your official statement, and move on. The only exception is comments containing threats, hate speech, or personally identifiable information about victims -- these should be removed under platform terms of service.

### Consider a Dedicated Crisis Page or Hashtag

For major crises, consider creating a dedicated page or hashtag where all official updates will be shared. This centralises the conversation and makes it easier for the public and media to find accurate information. Pin the post or link to it from your bio/header across all platforms.

## Phase 2: The Volume Strategy for Reputation Recovery

Once the immediate crisis is stabilised, the focus shifts from containment to recovery. Most organisations fail here -- they assume that once the news cycle moves on, the crisis is over. It is not. Search results and social media archives persist far longer than headlines.

### The Core Principle: Out-Produce, Do Not Out-Debate

You cannot win a reputation battle by arguing with critics. You cannot delete your way to a clean slate. You cannot wait for negative sentiment to fade on its own -- it will be refreshed every time someone searches your brand name and finds crisis coverage on the first page of results.

The only reliable strategy is volume. You must out-produce negative content with positive content -- a sustained flow of authentic material about your brand, your people, and your actions. Over time, this pushes negative content down in search results and reshapes the social media conversation.

### Create Positive New Content About the Brand

Develop a content strategy specifically designed for reputation recovery. This should include:

- **Behind-the-scenes content** showing your team, your operations, and your culture.
- **Customer testimonials and success stories** (with permission) demonstrating the positive experiences people have with your brand.
- **Community involvement** highlighting charitable work, local partnerships, and social responsibility initiatives.
- **Thought leadership** positioning your senior team as experts and responsible voices in your industry.
- **Product and service updates** showcasing improvements, innovations, and new offerings.
- **Visual content** including high-quality images and video, which consistently drives higher engagement across all platforms.

Every piece of content should be genuine. Audiences detect inauthenticity instantly, and during a post-crisis period your credibility is under heightened scrutiny.

### Engage Users to Turn the Tide of Conversation

Content alone is not enough. Actively engage with your audience to shift the conversation:

- Respond to every positive comment with genuine thanks.
- Ask questions that invite positive responses.
- Share user-generated content that presents the brand positively.
- Run campaigns or competitions that generate positive engagement.
- Collaborate with influencers and advocates who genuinely support the brand.

The goal is to make the positive conversation louder and more visible than the negative one. This is not about suppressing criticism -- it is about ensuring criticism does not define you.

## Phase 3: Sustained Daily Monitoring and Publishing

Reputation recovery on social media is not a sprint. The Coral Sea case study demonstrated that it takes a minimum of 12 months of sustained, consistent effort to achieve meaningful results. This phase requires daily discipline.

### Daily Monitoring Protocol

Every day during the recovery period, your team should:

- Review all comments, shares, and likes across all platforms.
- Identify any resurgence of negative sentiment or new crisis triggers.
- Respond to all comments within two hours during business hours.
- Flag media inquiries or influencer mentions for the communications team.

### Consistent Publishing Schedule

Establish a publishing schedule and stick to it. Consistency builds trust and keeps your channels active in platform algorithms. Recommended minimum cadence:

- **Facebook:** 1 post per day plus Stories.
- **Twitter/X:** 2-3 posts per day.
- **Instagram:** 1 post per day plus Stories and Reels.
- **LinkedIn:** 3-4 posts per week.
- **YouTube:** 1-2 videos per week.

Never go silent. A dormant channel during recovery signals that you have given up.

### Visual Content Drives Higher Engagement

Visual content -- images, video, infographics -- consistently outperforms text-only posts. Invest in professional photography, short-form video (30-90 seconds), longer YouTube content documenting recovery actions, and infographics communicating positive achievements. The Coral Sea results showed YouTube achieving nearly 96% growth despite being the hardest platform to grow.

## Metrics That Matter

Track these metrics monthly to measure recovery progress:

- **Follower/subscriber growth** (percentage change from crisis baseline).
- **Engagement rate** (likes, comments, shares as a percentage of reach).
- **Sentiment ratio** (positive vs. negative mentions).
- **Share of voice** (your brand mentions vs. competitor mentions).
- **Search result composition** (positive vs. negative results in the first 20 for your brand).
- **Response time** (average time to respond to comments and inquiries).

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Going dark.** Pausing scheduled content is correct; going completely silent is not. Your channels should remain active with crisis-appropriate content.
- **Arguing with critics.** You will not win public arguments. Respond professionally and move on.
- **Deleting comments.** This almost always makes things worse. Leave negative comments in place and respond to them.
- **Stopping too soon.** Three months of effort is not enough. Commit to at least 12 months of sustained activity.
- **Ignoring platforms.** A crisis will appear on platforms you do not normally use. Monitor everything, not just your primary channels.
- **Posting without approval.** During a crisis, every social media post should be reviewed and approved by the crisis management team before publication.

## Social Media Crisis Management Checklist

### Immediate (First 24 Hours)
- [ ] All scheduled content paused across all platforms.
- [ ] Monitoring activated on all channels.
- [ ] Holding statement adapted for social media and posted.
- [ ] Response template approved for replying to inquiries.
- [ ] Dedicated crisis page or pinned post created if appropriate.
- [ ] Team briefed: no unauthorised posting or engagement.

### Short-Term (First 2 Weeks)
- [ ] Daily monitoring protocol established.
- [ ] Content strategy for recovery phase drafted.
- [ ] Visual content production initiated.
- [ ] Engagement strategy defined.
- [ ] Metrics baseline established.

### Long-Term (12-Month Recovery)
- [ ] Consistent daily publishing schedule in place.
- [ ] Monthly metrics reporting to leadership.
- [ ] Quarterly strategy review and adjustment.
- [ ] Ongoing content creation pipeline maintained.
- [ ] Community engagement programme active.

Social media during a crisis is the primary battleground for your reputation. Treat it with the seriousness and sustained commitment it demands, and the evidence shows you can emerge stronger than before the crisis began.
