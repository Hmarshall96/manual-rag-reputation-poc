# How to Activate a Crisis Communications Plan

A crisis communications plan is only as good as the moment it is activated. When a reputation-threatening event strikes, the difference between a contained incident and a full-blown brand disaster often comes down to speed, structure, and discipline in the first few hours. This guide provides a step-by-step framework for activating a crisis plan, drawn from real-world crisis SOPs including the Coral Sea Resorts case study.

## Why Structure Matters in a Crisis

The single most important principle to understand is this: crisis is emotionally charged, and emotions drive poor decisions. People under pressure skip steps, contradict each other publicly, and react rather than respond. A structured crisis plan removes the guesswork and gives every team member a clear role, a clear chain of command, and a clear set of actions. Best practice guidance is not optional during a crisis -- it is the only thing standing between your organisation and reputational freefall.

## Step 1: Assess Severity Immediately

The first action upon becoming aware of a potential crisis is to assess its severity. Not every negative event requires full crisis activation. Use a three-tier classification:

- **Tier 1 (Low):** Localised issue, limited public awareness, no immediate safety risk. Examples include a single negative review going viral, a minor service failure, or an overbooking situation.
- **Tier 2 (Medium):** Significant operational disruption, moderate public awareness, potential for escalation. Examples include food safety incidents, facility failures affecting multiple customers, or a negative media inquiry.
- **Tier 3 (High):** Life-threatening situations, major legal exposure, widespread public attention. Examples include guest injury or death, fire or significant property damage, infectious disease outbreaks, or criminal allegations.

For Tier 3 incidents, activate the full crisis plan immediately and trigger the emergency notification chain. For Tier 1 and 2, the crisis management team lead should make an activation decision within 30 minutes of first awareness.

## Step 2: Designate the Crisis Management Team

Every organisation should have a pre-identified crisis management team (CMT). When the plan is activated, the following roles must be confirmed and briefed:

- **Crisis Lead:** Senior decision-maker with authority to approve all communications and operational responses.
- **Designated Spokesperson:** The single voice who will handle all external communications. This person must be media-trained.
- **Legal Advisor:** Available to review all statements before release and advise on liability exposure.
- **Operations Lead:** Manages the on-the-ground response, coordinates with emergency services if needed.
- **Communications/PR Lead:** Drafts all statements, monitors media, coordinates internal communications.
- **Digital/Social Media Lead:** Monitors and manages all social media channels during the crisis.

Each member should have a crisis pack containing contact lists, holding statement templates, scenario-specific action plans, and escalation procedures.

## Step 3: Establish Single-Voice Communication

This is non-negotiable. All external communication must flow through the designated spokesperson only. No other employee, board member, or stakeholder should speak to media, post on social media about the incident, or engage with public inquiries without explicit authorisation.

The chain of command works as follows:

1. All information flows to the Crisis Lead.
2. The Crisis Lead, in consultation with Legal and the Communications Lead, approves messaging.
3. The Designated Spokesperson delivers approved messaging to external audiences.
4. Internal communications are distributed separately through the Operations Lead.

Breaking this chain -- even once -- can create contradictory public statements that severely damage credibility.

## Step 4: Activate the Scenario-Specific Action Plan

Your crisis plan should contain pre-built action plans for every foreseeable scenario. At minimum, the following scenario types should be covered:

### Fire or Property Damage
- Immediate contact: emergency services, insurance provider, property management.
- PR action: holding statement confirming safety of guests/staff, cooperation with fire services.
- Key concern: guest welfare and business continuity.

### Food Safety Incidents
- Immediate contact: local health authority, food safety consultants, legal counsel.
- PR action: statement confirming investigation, voluntary product withdrawal if applicable.
- Key concern: public health, regulatory compliance.

### Guest Injury or Death
- Immediate contact: emergency services, legal counsel, next of kin (through police if fatality).
- PR action: holding statement expressing concern, confirming cooperation with authorities.
- Key concern: legal liability, compassionate communication, information sharing vs. legal investigation protection.

### Facility Failures
- Immediate contact: maintenance/engineering, health and safety officer, affected customers.
- PR action: statement on remedial actions, compensation or alternative arrangements.
- Key concern: customer retention, safety assurance.

### Overbooking or Service Failures
- Immediate contact: operations team, affected customers directly.
- PR action: apology and remediation offer, proactive outreach before complaints go public.
- Key concern: customer satisfaction, preventing social media escalation.

### Infectious Disease Outbreaks
- Immediate contact: public health authorities, medical professionals, legal counsel.
- PR action: statement confirming cooperation with health authorities, detailing precautionary measures.
- Key concern: public safety, regulatory reporting obligations, business continuity.

For each scenario, the pre-built action plan should specify who the spokesperson is (it may vary by scenario type), who the immediate contacts are, and what the first three PR actions should be.

## Step 5: Trigger the Notification Chain

For life-threatening crises (Tier 3), the notification chain must be activated within minutes, not hours. The chain should follow this sequence:

1. Emergency services (if not already contacted).
2. Crisis Lead and Legal Advisor.
3. Designated Spokesperson and Communications Lead.
4. Board or senior leadership.
5. Insurance and external crisis consultants if retained.
6. Relevant regulatory bodies (as legally required).

Every member of the notification chain should have primary and backup contact methods documented and tested quarterly.

## Step 6: Information Sharing vs. Legal Protection

One of the most delicate aspects of crisis activation is balancing the public's right to information with the organisation's need to protect itself legally. The general principles are:

- **Share:** Confirmed facts, actions being taken, expressions of concern, commitment to transparency.
- **Protect:** Speculation about causes, admission of fault before investigation, personal details of affected individuals, internal investigation findings.

Legal counsel should review every external statement before release. However, legal review must not cause unacceptable delays. Pre-approved holding statement templates (see H16) help bridge this gap.

## Step 7: Conduct an On-Site Audit

If the crisis involves a physical location, an on-site audit is critical. You cannot manage a crisis effectively from a boardroom if you do not understand the physical reality on the ground. The audit should cover:

- Current state of the site and any damage or hazards.
- Witness accounts from staff and affected parties.
- Photographic and video documentation (for internal use, not public release without legal approval).
- Identification of any ongoing risks or secondary incidents.

This information feeds directly into your communications and helps ensure that public statements are accurate.

## Step 8: Prepare Media Communication Guidelines

Your crisis plan should include media communication guidelines that cover every possible scenario. These guidelines should specify:

- Approved key messages for each scenario type.
- Phrases and terminology to use and to avoid.
- Procedures for handling ambush interviews or unexpected media contact.
- Social media response protocols.
- Escalation procedures if media pressure intensifies.

The designated spokesperson should rehearse these guidelines regularly, not just during a live crisis.

## The Two Phases of Crisis Response

Crisis management operates in two distinct phases, and your plan must address both:

### Phase 1: Immediate Containment (Hours to Days)
- Activate the crisis plan and notification chain.
- Issue holding statements.
- Manage media inquiries.
- Stabilise operations.
- Protect affected individuals.
- Document everything.

### Phase 2: Long-Term Recovery (Weeks to 12 Months)
- Implement a sustained reputation recovery programme.
- Rebuild trust through consistent positive actions and communications.
- Monitor sentiment across all channels.
- Conduct a post-crisis review and update the crisis plan.
- Train staff on lessons learned.
- Publish progress updates to demonstrate accountability.

The Coral Sea Resorts case study demonstrated that a structured 12-month recovery programme, combining proactive PR, social media engagement, and content creation, can fully restore and even improve a brand's reputation after a severe crisis.

## Crisis Plan Activation Checklist

Use this checklist within the first 60 minutes of crisis identification:

- [ ] Severity assessed and tier classification assigned.
- [ ] Crisis Management Team notified and briefed.
- [ ] Designated Spokesperson confirmed and available.
- [ ] Legal Advisor engaged.
- [ ] Scenario-specific action plan identified and activated.
- [ ] Notification chain triggered (Tier 3: within minutes).
- [ ] Holding statement issued or ready for release (see H16).
- [ ] Social media channels monitored, scheduled content paused (see H17).
- [ ] On-site audit initiated (if applicable).
- [ ] Media communication guidelines distributed to all CMT members.
- [ ] Internal communications sent to staff with approved messaging.
- [ ] Documentation of all actions and decisions begun.

Preparation is everything. The time to build your crisis plan is before you need it. Test it, drill it, update it, and when the moment comes, activate it with confidence.
