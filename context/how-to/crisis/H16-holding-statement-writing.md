# How to Write a Holding Statement for Media

A holding statement is the first public communication your organisation releases during a crisis. Its purpose is simple but vital: buy time while facts are gathered and control the initial narrative before speculation fills the vacuum. A well-crafted holding statement demonstrates that you are aware, in control, and acting responsibly. A poor one -- or worse, silence -- allows others to define the story for you.

## Why Holding Statements Matter

In the first hours of a crisis, journalists, customers, employees, and regulators all want answers. The problem is that you rarely have complete answers at that point. A holding statement bridges the gap between awareness and full understanding. It tells the world three things:

1. You know what is happening.
2. You are taking it seriously.
3. You will share more information when you have it.

Without a holding statement, media outlets will report that you were "unavailable for comment," which the public almost universally interprets as guilt or evasion.

## The Core Structure of a Holding Statement

Every effective holding statement follows a four-part structure, regardless of the nature of the crisis:

### 1. Acknowledge Awareness
Confirm that you know about the incident. This immediately signals that you are not hiding from the situation.

### 2. Express Concern
Show genuine empathy for anyone affected. This must come across as human and sincere, not corporate or rehearsed.

### 3. State Actions Being Taken
Briefly describe what you are doing in response. This demonstrates control and responsibility.

### 4. Promise Updates
Commit to sharing further information as it becomes available. This gives you a legitimate reason to end the conversation without answering questions you cannot yet answer.

## Ready-to-Use Holding Statement Templates

The following templates should be pre-drafted, reviewed by legal counsel, and stored in your crisis communications pack so they can be deployed within minutes of a crisis emerging. Customise the bracketed sections to fit the specific incident.

### Template 1: Generic Holding Statement

Use this for incidents where facts are still being established and the severity is not yet fully understood.

> "We are aware of [incident/situation] at [location/involving our organisation]. The safety and wellbeing of [our customers/guests/employees/the public] is our top priority. We are working closely with [relevant authorities/emergency services/regulatory bodies] to understand the situation fully. We will provide further information as it becomes available."

**When to use:** Initial reports of an incident, early-stage situations where you need to acknowledge awareness without committing to details you do not yet have.

### Template 2: Serious Incident Holding Statement

Use this when the incident is confirmed, particularly for situations involving injury, death, significant property damage, or legal exposure.

> "We can confirm that [brief factual statement about what has occurred]. We are cooperating fully with [relevant authorities/police/health and safety executive/regulatory body]. Our thoughts are with [those affected/the families involved/our employees]. We have activated our crisis response procedures and will provide updates as more information becomes available."

**When to use:** Confirmed serious incidents where some facts are established. This template acknowledges the gravity of the situation while maintaining appropriate boundaries around detail.

## Key Principles for Writing Holding Statements

### Be Factual
Only include information you have confirmed. Every word in a holding statement can and will be scrutinised. If you state something that later proves inaccurate, your credibility will be significantly damaged. When in doubt, leave it out.

### Do Not Speculate
Never offer theories about causes, prevention, or consequences. Speculation creates expectations, implies knowledge you do not have, and can be used against you legally.

### Do Not Assign Blame
Even if you believe you know who is responsible, a holding statement is not the place to say so. Premature blame can expose you to defamation claims and undermine ongoing investigations.

### Show Empathy
The public judges organisations on how human they sound during a crisis. Phrases like "our thoughts are with" and "the safety and wellbeing of our [stakeholders] is our top priority" are not cliches in this context -- they are essential signals of compassion. However, they must be delivered with sincerity. If your holding statement reads like it was written by a legal team with no human input, it will backfire.

### Promise Transparency
Committing to providing updates as information becomes available is not just a closing line. It is a contract with your audience. You must follow through. If you promise updates and then go silent, trust will erode rapidly. Set a realistic cadence for updates (e.g., "we will provide a further update by [time]") and stick to it, even if the update is simply that the investigation is ongoing.

## Adapting Tone for Different Audiences

A holding statement is not one-size-fits-all. While the core facts remain the same, the tone and emphasis should shift depending on who you are addressing.

### Media
Tone: professional, measured, cooperative. Media want facts, timelines, and access. Your holding statement for media should be concise, quotable, and free of jargon. Treat media contact as an opportunity, not a threat. A journalist who receives a prompt, professional holding statement is far more likely to report fairly than one who is stonewalled.

### Customers
Tone: empathetic, reassuring, action-oriented. Customers want to know they are safe, that you care, and what you are doing to fix the situation. Lead with empathy, follow with action. If customers are directly affected, provide practical information (alternative arrangements, refund processes, support contact details).

### Employees
Tone: honest, supportive, directive. Employees need to know what has happened, what is expected of them, and where to direct external inquiries. Internal holding statements should include clear instructions: do not speak to media, direct all inquiries to the designated spokesperson, continue normal operations unless instructed otherwise. Employees who feel informed and included are far less likely to become sources of leaks or misinformation.

### Regulators
Tone: compliant, cooperative, thorough. Regulatory communications should demonstrate that you are meeting your legal obligations, cooperating with investigations, and taking appropriate remedial action. These communications often require legal review and may need to follow specific formats or reporting requirements.

## Pre-Approval by Legal Counsel

Holding statement templates should be reviewed and pre-approved by your legal team before any crisis occurs. This is critically important because:

- During a live crisis, legal review can cause delays of hours. Pre-approved templates can be deployed in minutes.
- Legal counsel can identify language that creates unnecessary liability exposure.
- Pre-approval ensures that the communications team and the legal team are aligned on what can and cannot be said.

The pre-approval process should cover both templates and a set of approved phrases and terminology for common scenario types. This gives the communications team the flexibility to customise statements without waiting for legal sign-off on every word.

Review and re-approve holding statement templates at least annually, or whenever there is a significant change in your organisation's risk profile.

## Media Relations: The Single Spokesperson Rule

Your holding statement must come from, and only from, your designated spokesperson. This person should be:

- Senior enough to carry authority.
- Media-trained and comfortable under pressure.
- Briefed on the crisis plan, the holding statement, and the approved key messages.
- Available and contactable for follow-up inquiries.

No other member of staff should engage with media under any circumstances during a crisis. This includes social media. Well-meaning employees who try to help by responding to journalists can inadvertently contradict the official position or reveal sensitive information.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **"No comment."** This phrase is universally interpreted as having something to hide. Always provide a holding statement, even if it contains minimal detail.
- **Over-promising.** Do not promise outcomes you cannot guarantee. Saying "we will ensure this never happens again" before you understand what happened sets you up for failure.
- **Defensive language.** Phrases like "we deny any wrongdoing" or "we reject these allegations" sound adversarial and guilty. A holding statement should be cooperative in tone.
- **Delayed release.** A holding statement loses its value if it arrives hours after the story has broken. Aim to release within 30 to 60 minutes of becoming aware of the crisis.
- **Inconsistency.** If your holding statement says one thing and your social media says another, you have a bigger problem than the original crisis. Ensure all channels carry the same message.

## Holding Statement Checklist

Before releasing any holding statement, verify that it meets these criteria:

- [ ] Acknowledges awareness of the incident.
- [ ] Expresses genuine concern for those affected.
- [ ] States specific actions being taken.
- [ ] Commits to providing further updates.
- [ ] Contains only confirmed facts.
- [ ] Does not speculate on causes or consequences.
- [ ] Does not assign blame.
- [ ] Has been reviewed or is based on a pre-approved template.
- [ ] Is consistent with messaging on all other channels.
- [ ] Has been approved for release by the Crisis Lead.
- [ ] Will be delivered by the designated spokesperson only.

A holding statement is not the end of your crisis communication -- it is the beginning. Get it right, and you establish credibility and control that will serve you throughout the crisis and recovery.
