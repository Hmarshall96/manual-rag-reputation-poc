# How to Conduct a New Client Reputation Assessment

The initial client assessment is the most consequential step in a reputation management engagement. It determines the strategy, sets the timeline, defines success, and establishes realistic expectations. A thorough assessment prevents two of the most common causes of client dissatisfaction: overpromising on outcomes and misunderstanding the true scope of the reputation problem. This guide covers the full assessment process from first contact through to strategy proposal.

## The 5-Phase Sales and Onboarding Process

Before diving into the assessment methodology, understand where it fits in the broader client engagement lifecycle:

1. **Qualification.** Initial conversation to determine whether the enquiry is a fit for your services. Not every reputation issue is manageable, and not every prospect has the budget or commitment required.
2. **Initial opinion.** A preliminary review of the reputation landscape, typically conducted before a formal proposal. This is where the assessment described in this guide takes place.
3. **Proposal.** A detailed document outlining the recommended strategy, deliverables, timeline, and pricing based on the assessment findings.
4. **Kick-off.** Formal campaign launch with the client, including introduction of the delivery team, agreement on communication protocols, and confirmation of goals.
5. **Delivery.** Ongoing execution of the strategy with regular reporting and adjustment.

The assessment is the foundation of phases 2 and 3. Get it right and the rest of the engagement flows smoothly. Get it wrong and you will be managing client expectations problems for the duration of the contract.

## Phase 1: Fact-Finding Questions

The first step is a structured conversation with the prospective client. Use the following questions as a framework, adapting the tone and depth to the specific situation.

### Understanding the Problem

- **What is the specific reputation issue you are facing?** Let the client describe it in their own words first. Listen for whether the problem is a single negative article, a pattern of bad reviews, a specific antagonist, or a broader positioning challenge.
- **When did the issue start, and what triggered it?** Understanding the timeline and origin helps assess whether the situation is acute (a single event) or chronic (ongoing). It also reveals whether the trigger is something that could recur.
- **What search terms surface the negative content?** Ask the client to share the exact searches they have performed. This reveals their perspective on the problem and identifies the priority keywords for your own audit.
- **Have any removal attempts been made already?** This is critical. Previous failed removal requests (especially legal ones) can complicate future efforts. Previous contact with publishers or platforms needs to be understood before you take any action.

### Understanding the Desired Outcome

- **What is the desired outcome?** The three main categories are:
  - **Removal:** Complete elimination of the negative content from the internet.
  - **Suppression:** Pushing negative content off page one of Google so it is rarely seen.
  - **Repositioning:** Changing the overall narrative from negative to positive or neutral.

  Most campaigns involve a combination of these. Help the client articulate which matters most.

- **What does success look like to you in 6 months? In 12 months?** This question surfaces unrealistic expectations early. If a client expects all negative content to be removed within 30 days, it is better to address that now than after they have signed a contract.

### Understanding Existing Assets

- **What positive content or assets already exist?** Ask about:
  - Company website (quality, authority, freshness)
  - Active social media profiles
  - Existing positive press coverage
  - Blog or content marketing programme
  - Industry awards or recognitions
  - Professional speaking engagements
  - Published books, articles, or research

  Existing assets determine how quickly you can begin suppression. A client with a strong website and active LinkedIn is in a much better starting position than one with no online presence at all.

### Practical Considerations

- **What is the timeline expectation?** Reputation management is not instant. Removal can sometimes be achieved in weeks but suppression typically takes 3-12 months depending on the strength of the negative content. Set realistic frames from the first conversation.
- **What is the budget range?** It is better to discuss budget early than to spend time producing a proposal that is far above or below the client's expectations.
- **Are there legal proceedings ongoing or planned?** Legal activity can both help and hinder reputation management. A court order for removal is a powerful tool. But ongoing litigation may mean certain actions (such as contacting publishers) need to be coordinated with lawyers.
- **Who are the key stakeholders?** Identify the decision-maker, the day-to-day contact, and anyone else who needs to approve content or strategy. Multi-stakeholder environments slow down execution if not managed from the start.

## Phase 2: The Search Audit

This is the technical core of the assessment. You are mapping the current state of the client's online reputation in Google search results.

### Search Process

Perform all searches in a clean browser environment: incognito/private browsing mode, logged out of all Google accounts, with location set to the client's primary market (UK, US, etc.).

Search for the following:

1. **Exact name** (in quotes): "Company Name" or "Person Name"
2. **Name without quotes:** Company Name or Person Name
3. **Name variations:** Abbreviations, former names, common misspellings
4. **Name + negative qualifiers:** "Company Name" scam, "Company Name" reviews, "Company Name" complaints, "Company Name" fraud
5. **Key executive names** (if corporate client): same variations as above

For each search, record every result on pages 1 through 3 (approximately 30 results). Note the following for each:

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Position | Rank on the page (1-10 per page) |
| Title | The title tag of the result |
| URL | Full URL including domain |
| Source type | News site, blog, forum, social profile, review site, directory, corporate site |
| Sentiment | Positive, Neutral, or Negative |
| Date | When the content was published or last updated |

### Google Verticals

Do not limit your search to the main organic results. Also check:

- **Google Images.** Negative images (unflattering photos, protest images, memes) can appear in the image carousel on the main search page.
- **Google News.** Recent negative news coverage may appear in the "Top stories" section.
- **Google Videos.** YouTube videos and other video content about the brand.
- **People Also Ask.** Check what questions Google auto-suggests. Questions like "Is [Company Name] a scam?" indicate established negative search behaviour.
- **Autocomplete suggestions.** Start typing the brand name and note what Google suggests. Negative autocomplete suggestions (e.g., "Company Name lawsuit") are visible to everyone who begins to search for the brand.

## Phase 3: Classify and Assess Each Result

### Classification

For each result recorded in the audit, classify it:

- **Positive:** Content that portrays the brand or individual favourably. Owned website, positive press, good reviews, professional profiles.
- **Neutral:** Content that is factual and neither positive nor negative. Company directory listings, factual news coverage, Wikipedia entries with balanced content.
- **Negative:** Content that damages the brand or individual. Negative articles, bad reviews, complaint forum posts, legal records, negative social media posts.

### Assess Removability

For each negative result, assess the feasibility of removal:

- **High removability:** Content that violates platform terms of service, contains provably false statements (defamation), is outdated and qualifies for right to be forgotten (in applicable jurisdictions), or is on a site known to respond to removal requests.
- **Medium removability:** Content where removal is possible but uncertain. May require legal action, negotiation with the publisher, or a formal complaint process.
- **Low removability:** Content on high-authority news sites with editorial independence, court records, government publications, or major platforms that rarely remove content. These typically require suppression rather than removal.

This classification directly shapes the strategy. If all negative results have low removability, the strategy will focus almost entirely on content creation and suppression. If some have high removability, the strategy will prioritise those quick wins first.

## Phase 4: Compile the Assessment Report

The assessment report is the deliverable from this process and the foundation of the proposal. Structure it as follows:

### Executive Summary

A one-page overview: what the reputation problem is, how severe it is, and what the recommended approach is. Write this for a non-technical audience. The decision-maker may only read this page.

### Current State Analysis

- Summary of search audit findings with a table or visual showing the positive/neutral/negative breakdown across pages 1-3.
- Screenshots of key negative results.
- Autocomplete and People Also Ask findings.

### Detailed Result Classification

Full table of all results with position, URL, sentiment classification, and removability assessment.

### Recommended Strategy

Based on the findings, outline the recommended approach:

- **Removal actions** for high-removability negative results (with estimated timelines).
- **Suppression plan** including content creation, profile optimisation, and SEO strategy (with estimated timelines).
- **Monitoring setup** to track progress and detect new threats.
- **Ongoing management** including social media, review generation, and content publishing.

### Timeline and Milestones

Set realistic expectations:

- **Weeks 1-4:** Removal requests submitted, monitoring established, quick wins actioned.
- **Months 1-3:** Content creation and optimisation underway, initial SERP movement expected.
- **Months 3-6:** Significant suppression progress visible, ongoing content and SEO work.
- **Months 6-12:** Consolidation and maintenance, monitoring for new threats.

Some negatives can be removed in weeks. Suppression of strong negative content from authoritative sources typically takes 6-12 months. Never promise faster timelines than the evidence supports.

### Investment

Present pricing clearly, tied to the scope of work outlined in the strategy. Where possible, offer tiered options (essential, recommended, comprehensive) so the client can select the level of investment that matches their budget and urgency.

## Setting Expectations

The assessment conversation is the best opportunity to set expectations that will prevent disappointment later:

- **Not everything can be removed.** Be honest about which content is likely to remain and explain the suppression alternative.
- **Results take time.** SEO-based suppression does not happen overnight. Prepare the client for a 6-12 month engagement.
- **Ongoing management is usually necessary.** Reputation is not a one-time fix. New content appears, search algorithms change, and competitors evolve. Most clients benefit from ongoing monitoring and maintenance after the initial campaign.
- **The client's participation matters.** They will need to provide information, approve content, and potentially participate in thought leadership activities. A passive client gets slower results.

## Checklist Summary

- [ ] Conduct structured fact-finding interview with prospect
- [ ] Perform full Google search audit across all name variations and verticals
- [ ] Record all results on pages 1-3 with position, URL, source, and sentiment
- [ ] Check Google Images, News, Videos, Autocomplete, and People Also Ask
- [ ] Classify every result as positive, neutral, or negative
- [ ] Assess removability of each negative result
- [ ] Compile assessment report with executive summary
- [ ] Develop recommended strategy with realistic timelines
- [ ] Present tiered pricing options
- [ ] Set clear expectations about timelines, outcomes, and client involvement
