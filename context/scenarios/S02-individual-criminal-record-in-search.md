# Individual with Criminal Record in Search Results

## Situation Overview

An individual's past criminal conviction is appearing in Google search results when their name is searched. The conviction may be documented in news articles, court record databases, or public registers. In many cases the conviction is old and the individual has moved on, yet the persistent visibility is causing ongoing harm — preventing employment, damaging personal relationships, and undermining reintegration into society.

This is one of the most personally distressing reputation scenarios. The individual often feels trapped by their past and may have already made unsuccessful attempts at removal through informal channels.

## Key Characteristics

- The conviction may be "spent" under the Rehabilitation of Offenders Act 1974 (UK), meaning the individual is legally entitled not to disclose it.
- News articles report the conviction, often with full name and photograph.
- Court record aggregation sites or mugshot databases may also host the information.
- The individual is typically not a public figure, which strengthens the case for de-listing.
- The content ranks prominently — often as the first or second result for their name.
- Employment is the most common area of impact, but housing, relationships, and community standing are also affected.

## Recommended Actions

### Step 1: Determine Conviction Status (Week 1)

Establish whether the conviction is spent under the Rehabilitation of Offenders Act. The rehabilitation period depends on sentence length and type. Once spent, the individual has significantly stronger grounds for removal and de-listing.

### Step 2: PNC Record Deletion (Weeks 1-4)

If the conviction is spent, apply for deletion of the Police National Computer record (see H12). Write to the relevant police force's Records Management department, citing the Data Protection Act 2018. While PNC deletion does not directly remove online content, it supports the broader case and can be referenced in RTBF applications.

### Step 3: Right to Be Forgotten Application (Weeks 2-6)

Submit an RTBF request to Google for de-listing of relevant URLs (see H01). The application should emphasise that the conviction is spent, the individual is not a public figure, the information is no longer in the public interest, and continued availability causes disproportionate harm. RTBF applications for spent convictions have a reasonable success rate, particularly for private citizens with non-serious offences.

### Step 4: Direct Removal Requests (Weeks 2-8)

In parallel, contact publishers directly. Local news outlets may remove or anonymise old articles when presented with evidence the conviction is spent. For court record aggregation sites, follow each site's specific removal process.

### Step 5: Positive Content Strategy (Months 2-6)

Build a positive online presence to compete with remaining negative results (see A07): optimise a LinkedIn profile, build a personal website if appropriate, establish positive social media profiles, and pursue opportunities for published achievements or community involvement.

Refer to case study CS02 for a successful campaign combining RTBF, direct removal, and content suppression.

## Risks and Considerations

**RTBF rejection.** Google may refuse if the conviction is considered relevant to public safety or the offence was serious. Prepare an appeal strategy, potentially through the ICO.

**Multiple sources.** The conviction may appear on numerous sites, each requiring separate removal efforts.

**Re-publication risk.** Content can be re-published or cached versions may persist. Ongoing monitoring is essential.

**Emotional sensitivity.** This scenario requires careful, empathetic handling. The individual is often in genuine distress with unrealistic expectations about timescales.

## Success Metrics

- Conviction-related content de-listed from Google results for the individual's name.
- PNC record reviewed or deleted where applicable.
- Positive content occupies page one of search results.
- Individual reports improved outcomes in employment and personal life.
