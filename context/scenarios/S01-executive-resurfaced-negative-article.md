# Executive Facing Resurfaced Negative Article

## Situation Overview

An old negative news article about an executive has resurfaced or is ranking highly in search engine results when their name is searched. The article may relate to past legal proceedings, workplace disputes, previous business failures, regulatory actions, or personal matters that the executive considered resolved long ago. Despite the passage of time, the article has regained prominence — possibly due to algorithm changes, renewed media interest, or increased search volume triggered by a new appointment or business venture.

This scenario is particularly damaging when the executive is actively seeking new roles, pursuing board appointments, pitching to investors, or entering new business partnerships. Decision-makers routinely search candidates online, and a prominently ranked negative article can derail opportunities before a conversation even begins.

## Key Characteristics

- The article originates from a credible or semi-credible news source, making it harder to dismiss or remove.
- It ranks on page one of Google for the executive's name, often in the top three to five results.
- The executive has a limited positive online presence, meaning the negative result faces little competition in search rankings.
- The content may be outdated, lack context, or omit the resolution of the matter it describes (e.g. charges dropped, dispute settled, business recovered).
- The executive is entering a phase where online perception directly affects professional outcomes — job searches, fundraising, public-facing roles.

## Recommended Actions

### Immediate Assessment (Week 1)

Begin with a thorough SERP audit to understand the full extent of the problem. Document every result on pages one and two of Google for the executive's name, noting domain authority, content type, and sentiment. Identify whether the article is the only negative result or part of a wider pattern.

Assess removability. If the executive is based in the UK or EU, evaluate whether a Right to Be Forgotten (RTBF) application to Google is viable (see H01). Key factors include whether the content is outdated, whether the executive is a public figure, and whether the information remains in the public interest. If the article contains factual inaccuracies or is defamatory, explore legal avenues for removal or correction (see H03). Contact the publication directly if there are grounds for amendment, such as a missing outcome to legal proceedings.

### Short-Term Suppression (Weeks 2-8)

If removal is unlikely or will take time, begin a content suppression strategy in parallel (see A02). The goal is to push the negative article off page one by creating and optimising competing content that ranks for the same search terms. This typically involves:

- Optimising the executive's LinkedIn profile with keyword-rich content matching their name.
- Creating or improving a personal website or portfolio site.
- Publishing thought leadership articles, interviews, or guest posts on high-authority platforms.
- Building out professional profiles on relevant industry directories and platforms.
- Securing positive media coverage through targeted PR activity (see H18).

Refer to case studies CS02 and CS03 for examples of successful suppression campaigns for executives in similar situations.

### Ongoing Brand Building (Months 3-12+)

Sustainable reputation repair requires more than short-term suppression. Develop an ongoing personal branding strategy (see A07) that establishes the executive as an authority in their field. This includes regular content publication, speaking engagements, podcast appearances, and social media activity that generates a steady stream of positive, indexable content.

Set up continuous monitoring (see A02) to track ranking changes, detect any new negative content early, and measure the effectiveness of suppression efforts over time.

## Risks and Considerations

**RTBF may be refused.** Google rejects approximately 71% of RTBF requests. Content about executives in senior roles is more likely to be deemed in the public interest, particularly if it relates to corporate governance, financial misconduct, or regulatory action. Prepare the client for this possibility and ensure suppression runs in parallel rather than depending on removal.

**Streisand effect.** If the executive pursues legal action against the publisher and this becomes public, it can generate additional negative coverage and draw more attention to the original article. Legal approaches should be considered carefully and used only where there are strong grounds.

**Timelines.** Suppression campaigns for high-authority news articles typically take three to six months to show meaningful results. Executives should be given realistic expectations from the outset.

**Disclosure considerations.** In some regulated industries, executives may have disclosure obligations regarding past legal or regulatory matters. Reputation management activity should not conflict with these requirements.

## Success Metrics

- Negative article moves from page one to page two or beyond.
- Positive, controlled content occupies the majority of page one results.
- Executive reports improved outcomes in professional interactions and opportunities.
- Monitoring shows stable or improving SERP position over time.
