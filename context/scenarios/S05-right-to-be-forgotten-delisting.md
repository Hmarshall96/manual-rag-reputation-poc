# Person Seeking Right to Be Forgotten De-listing

## Situation Overview

An individual wants outdated, irrelevant, or inaccurate content de-listed from Google search results under the Right to Be Forgotten (RTBF), established by the 2014 CJEU ruling in Google Spain v AEPD. In the UK, this right is preserved post-Brexit under the UK GDPR and Data Protection Act 2018. The individual may not be facing a crisis — they simply want old content that no longer reflects who they are to stop appearing when their name is searched.

RTBF does not remove content from the internet. It de-lists specific URLs from Google results for the individual's name. The content remains on the source website but becomes significantly harder to find.

## Key Characteristics

- The individual has identified URLs containing outdated or irrelevant personal information.
- Content may relate to old news, expired legal matters, historical financial difficulties, or outdated professional information.
- The individual is typically a private citizen rather than a public figure.
- Content ranks on page one for their name, causing ongoing harm.

## Relevant Statistics

Google's transparency data shows an overall acceptance rate of approximately 29%. Rates vary by content type:

- **Online directories:** 54.6% — the highest category.
- **Social media:** approximately 34%.
- **News articles:** significantly lower, particularly for public figures or public interest matters.

These figures highlight the importance of careful case assessment before submission.

## Recommended Actions

### Stage 1: Eligibility Assessment (Week 1)

Assess whether the content is likely to qualify (see H01). Key factors:

- **Time elapsed:** Greater age strengthens the case.
- **Public interest:** Content about serious crimes or financial misconduct is more likely to be refused.
- **Public figure status:** Politicians and senior executives face a higher threshold.
- **Accuracy:** Inaccurate content has stronger grounds.
- **Sensitivity:** Content relating to health, criminal history, or political beliefs.
- **Impact:** Demonstrable specific harm from continued availability.

### Stage 2: Prepare and Submit (Weeks 2-4)

Prepare the RTBF application via Google's form (see H01, H02). For each URL: identify the individual and their connection to the content, explain why it is outdated or irrelevant, describe specific harm, reference UK GDPR Article 17, and provide supporting evidence (proof of resolved legal matters, spent convictions, changed circumstances). Record the reference number.

### Stage 3: Handle the Response (Weeks 4-12)

Google typically responds within several weeks. Outcomes: accepted (verify de-listing), partially accepted (review refusal reasoning, consider revised submission), or rejected (consider ICO appeal — see H02).

### Stage 4: Appeal if Necessary (Months 3-6)

If rejected with strong grounds, file a complaint with the ICO. The ICO can investigate and order Google to de-list. This takes additional time but is a meaningful avenue for incorrect initial decisions.

### Stage 5: Complementary Suppression (Ongoing)

Regardless of outcome, consider content suppression to build a positive presence providing long-term protection against future negative content.

## Risks and Considerations

**Low acceptance rate.** At 29% overall, RTBF is not guaranteed. Careful preparation significantly improves the odds.

**Public interest defence.** Google frequently cites this for refusal, particularly for financial misconduct or professional malpractice content.

**Geographic limitations.** De-listing applies to searches within the relevant jurisdiction. Content may still appear from other regions.

**Not permanent.** Google can reverse decisions. Source content remains online and discoverable through other means.

## Success Metrics

- Target URLs de-listed from Google for the individual's name.
- ICO appeal successful where initial decision was unfavourable.
- Individual reports reduced negative impact on professional or personal life.
- Complementary suppression strategy in place for long-term protection.
