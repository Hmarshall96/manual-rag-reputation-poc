# Unwanted Personal Images or Content Indexed by Google

## Situation Overview

Personal or private images are appearing in Google Search or Google Images without the individual's consent. This may include photographs taken in private settings and shared without permission, images from old social media accounts the individual no longer controls, or personal content never intended for public visibility.

The content may have been uploaded by a former partner, an acquaintance, a hacker, or it may be old content resurfacing due to algorithm changes. This scenario ranges from mildly embarrassing to deeply distressing. In the most serious cases — intimate images shared without consent — criminal law protections may apply.

## Key Characteristics

- Images appear in Google Search or Google Images when the individual's name is searched.
- The individual did not consent to the images being published or indexed.
- Source sites may include social media platforms, image hosting sites, blogs, forums, or harassment sites.
- Images may be linked to the individual's name through metadata, alt text, or surrounding text.
- Impact ranges from professional embarrassment to severe emotional distress and safety concerns.

## Recommended Actions

### Immediate Assessment (Week 1)

Document all instances of the unwanted content — URLs, screenshots, and hosting platforms. Then determine the legal basis for removal:

- **Copyright (DMCA):** If the individual took the photograph (e.g. a selfie), they own the copyright. A DMCA takedown notice can be sent to the hosting platform and Google (see H05). This is often the fastest route.
- **Intimate images without consent:** In the UK, sharing private sexual images without consent is a criminal offence under the Criminal Justice and Courts Act 2015. Google has a specific, prioritised removal form for non-consensual intimate imagery.
- **Google's personal content removal policies:** Google allows removal of personally identifiable content creating risks of identity theft, fraud, or other harms (see H09).

### Direct Platform Removal (Weeks 1-3)

Contact hosting platforms directly to request removal. Major platforms have reporting mechanisms for non-consensual content, privacy violations, or copyright infringement. For less cooperative sites, a formal written request citing the Data Protection Act 2018, GDPR, or Copyright Designs and Patents Act 1988 may be necessary.

### Google Removal Requests (Weeks 1-4)

Submit removal requests using the appropriate Google form: DMCA for copyright-owned content (see H05), personal content removal for images under Google's updated policies (see H09), or RTBF for EU/UK individuals where images are outdated or excessive. Non-consensual intimate image requests are typically processed within days.

### Ongoing Monitoring (Months 2-6)

Set up Google Alerts and reverse image search monitoring to detect re-uploads. If content has been widely shared, repeated removal efforts may be needed across multiple sites. Build a positive online presence to ensure search results are dominated by content the individual controls (see A07).

## Risks and Considerations

**Re-upload risk.** Content can be re-uploaded after removal. Persistent monitoring is critical, especially with a determined harasser.

**Jurisdictional limitations.** Platforms outside the UK or EU may be less responsive to UK/European law. Focus on Google de-indexing where source removal fails.

**Emotional impact.** This is often highly emotionally charged. Handle communications with sensitivity and set realistic expectations.

**Evidence preservation.** Before requesting removal, document all evidence thoroughly. If criminal or civil proceedings may follow, the original content and hosting location are important evidence.

## Success Metrics

- All identified unwanted images removed from source platforms.
- Images de-indexed from Google Search and Google Images.
- Monitoring confirms no re-uploads within a sustained period.
- Individual reports feeling safer and more in control of their online presence.
