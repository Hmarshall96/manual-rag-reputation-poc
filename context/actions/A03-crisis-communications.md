# A03 - Crisis Communications Response

## Overview

A crisis communications response is required when a sudden, high-visibility event threatens a company's or individual's reputation and demands immediate action. Unlike ongoing reputation management, crisis communications operates under extreme time pressure. The first 24-48 hours define the narrative, and mistakes made early are exceptionally difficult to correct later.

This guide covers the strategic framework for managing a reputation crisis from initial detection through long-term recovery. It connects the immediate tactical steps — activating a crisis plan, issuing statements, managing social media — to the broader strategic arc of containment and rebuilding.

## Applicable Scenarios

- **S07 - Active corporate crisis**: Data breaches, product recalls, food safety incidents, environmental disasters, workplace accidents, executive misconduct, legal proceedings
- **S09 - Post-crisis reputation rebuilding**: The recovery phase after the immediate crisis has passed

## When to Activate Crisis Communications

Not every negative event is a crisis. A crisis is characterised by:

- **Urgency**: The situation is developing rapidly and demands an immediate response
- **Visibility**: Media, social media, or regulators are actively covering the event
- **Threat level**: The event poses material risk to the organisation's reputation, revenue, or operations
- **Uncertainty**: The full facts are not yet known
- **Escalation potential**: Without intervention, the situation will get worse

If these conditions are met, activate the crisis communications framework immediately. Delay is the single most damaging mistake in crisis management.

## Phase 1: Containment (Days 1-7)

The first phase is about stopping the bleeding. The goals are to establish control of the narrative, prevent speculation from filling the information vacuum, and demonstrate that the organisation is taking the situation seriously.

### Immediate Actions (Hours 0-4)

**Activate the crisis plan.** Every organisation should have a pre-prepared crisis communications plan. If one exists, activate it now. See **H15 - Crisis Communications Plan Activation** for the detailed process. If no plan exists, establish the following immediately:

1. **Designate a single spokesperson.** All external communications go through one person. This prevents contradictory messages and ensures consistency. The spokesperson should be senior enough to be credible but does not have to be the CEO — in fact, the CEO should be held in reserve for escalation.

2. **Assemble the crisis team.** This typically includes: the spokesperson, the CEO or MD, legal counsel, the communications lead, the operations lead relevant to the crisis, and the reputation management agency.

3. **Establish a facts-only briefing cadence.** The crisis team should meet every 2-4 hours during the acute phase to share verified facts, review media coverage, and agree on next communications.

### Issue a Holding Statement (Hours 2-8)

A holding statement buys time while you gather facts. It does not attempt to explain or resolve the situation — it simply acknowledges the event and commits to transparency. See **H16 - Drafting and Issuing a Holding Statement** for templates and guidance.

A good holding statement includes:

- Acknowledgement that the organisation is aware of the situation
- Confirmation that it is being taken seriously
- A commitment to investigate and share findings
- Empathy for anyone affected
- A clear next step ("we will provide an update by [time]")

A holding statement must never include speculation, blame, defensiveness, or promises that cannot be kept.

### Manage Social Media (Hours 0-72)

Social media is where crises accelerate. See **H17 - Social Media Crisis Management** for the tactical playbook. Key principles:

- **Monitor continuously.** Track all mentions, hashtags, and threads related to the crisis. Use social listening tools to catch emerging narratives.
- **Respond promptly but not hastily.** Acknowledge concerns quickly. Do not engage in arguments or respond to provocation.
- **Pause scheduled content.** Any pre-scheduled social media posts that are unrelated to the crisis should be paused immediately. A cheerful marketing post during a crisis is deeply damaging.
- **Direct enquiries to a single channel.** Funnel media and public enquiries to a dedicated email address or phone line.

### Days 2-7: Controlled Disclosure

As facts emerge, release them in a controlled, structured manner:

- **Provide regular updates** at pre-announced times. This reduces the incentive for journalists to speculate between updates.
- **Correct misinformation quickly.** If false claims are circulating, issue factual corrections promptly.
- **Announce concrete actions.** "We have suspended the product line pending investigation" is far more credible than "we are looking into it."
- **Show empathy throughout.** Every communication should lead with concern for those affected before addressing the organisation's position.

## Phase 2: Recovery (Months 1-12)

Once the acute crisis has passed, the focus shifts from containment to rebuilding. This is where reputation management techniques overlap with crisis communications.

### Month 1: Assessment and Strategy

1. **Conduct a full reputation audit** to understand the damage. See **A06 - Reputation Audit & Monitoring**. Map every negative article, social media thread, review, and search result that resulted from the crisis.

2. **Develop a recovery strategy.** This typically combines:
   - Content removal where possible (see **A01**) for content that is inaccurate or defamatory
   - SEO suppression of crisis-related content (see **A02**)
   - Positive content creation to rebuild the narrative (see **A02**, **A08**)
   - Review management if the crisis has affected review scores (see **A04**)

3. **Set realistic timelines.** A significant crisis typically takes 6-12 months to recover from in search results and longer in brand perception. Manage client expectations accordingly.

### Months 2-6: Active Rebuilding

4. **Execute the content strategy.** Begin publishing positive content, thought leadership pieces, and PR placements that demonstrate the lessons learned and changes made. See **H18 - PR Article Placement** and **H22 - Thought Leadership Content Creation**.

5. **Rebuild stakeholder relationships.** Direct outreach to key stakeholders — customers, partners, investors, employees — with a clear narrative about what happened, what changed, and what the organisation stands for now.

6. **Monitor the narrative.** Track search results, social media sentiment, and media coverage weekly. Adjust the strategy based on what is working.

### Months 6-12: Consolidation

7. **Continue content publication** at a steady cadence to maintain the positive trajectory.
8. **Transition from crisis recovery to proactive reputation management.** The goal is to reach a point where the crisis no longer dominates search results or public perception.
9. **Document lessons learned.** Update the crisis communications plan based on the experience.

## Key Principles Throughout

### Factual Communication Only

Never speculate. Never guess. Never provide information that has not been verified by the crisis team. "We don't yet have that information, but we will share it as soon as we do" is always better than an incorrect statement that must later be retracted.

### Empathy Before Explanation

Every public communication should begin by acknowledging the human impact of the crisis before explaining the organisation's position or actions. People need to feel heard before they will listen.

### Transparency Within Legal Limits

Share as much as legally possible. Silence is interpreted as guilt or indifference. However, work closely with legal counsel to ensure that communications do not create unnecessary legal exposure. There is a tension between legal caution and reputational necessity — this tension must be managed actively, not avoided.

### No Speculation About Others

Do not blame third parties, competitors, employees, or anyone else during a crisis. Even if the fault lies elsewhere, pointing fingers during a crisis looks defensive and untrustworthy. Accountability — even for things that are not entirely your fault — builds credibility.

### Single Source of Truth

All external communications must come from the designated spokesperson or approved channels. Every employee, board member, and partner should know that they are not authorised to speak publicly about the crisis and should direct all enquiries to the communications team.

## When Crisis Communications Fails

Crisis communications cannot fix everything. It will not work if:

- The organisation continues the behaviour that caused the crisis
- Leadership is unwilling to take accountability
- The facts are worse than initially disclosed and come out in stages (drip-feed disclosure is devastating)
- Internal communications are as poor as external ones, leading to leaks and contradictions

In these cases, the reputational damage will persist regardless of communications strategy, and the focus must shift to long-term suppression and rebuilding (see **A02**).
