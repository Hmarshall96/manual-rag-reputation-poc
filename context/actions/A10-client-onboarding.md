# A10 - Client Onboarding & Goal Setting

## Overview

Client onboarding is the process that transforms an initial enquiry into an effective, goal-oriented reputation management engagement. It is the most important phase of any project because it establishes the foundation for everything that follows — the strategy, the timeline, the budget, and the expectations. Done well, onboarding ensures that both the agency and the client are aligned on what success looks like and how to get there. Done poorly, it leads to misaligned expectations, wasted effort, and client dissatisfaction.

This guide covers the end-to-end onboarding process from first contact through to campaign kick-off, including fact-finding, reputation auditing, strategy recommendation, and goal setting.

## Applicable Scenarios

- **S11 - New client requiring initial assessment**: Every new engagement starts here

This guide also indirectly supports every other scenario, because it is the process through which the right combination of other action guides (A01-A09) is identified and deployed for each client.

## The Onboarding Process

### Step 1: Initial Fact-Finding

The first conversation with a prospective client must establish the essential facts of their situation. This is typically conducted as a discovery call or initial consultation.

**Key questions to ask:**

- **What prompted them to seek help now?** Understanding the trigger — a specific article, a Google search they did, a business impact they noticed — reveals the urgency and emotional state.
- **What content are they concerned about?** Get specific URLs. The client may describe their problem in general terms ("there's bad stuff about me online"), but you need the exact content to assess the situation.
- **What search terms are affected?** Which name, company, or keyword searches produce negative results? Have them share screenshots of what they see when they search.
- **What is the business or personal impact?** Lost job offers, declined business deals, personal distress, customer loss. This informs priority and budget.
- **What have they already tried?** Previous removal attempts, other agencies engaged, legal action taken. This prevents duplicating failed efforts.
- **What is their timeline expectation?** Clients often expect rapid results. Understanding their expectations early allows you to manage them before the engagement starts.
- **What is their budget range?** Reputation management costs vary enormously based on scope. Early budget alignment prevents wasted proposal effort.

**What to listen for:**

- Are they a private individual or a public figure? This affects RTBF eligibility and strategy options.
- Is the content factually true, false, or mixed? This determines whether removal or suppression is the primary path.
- Is there an ongoing dispute or legal matter? Active litigation may constrain what can be said or done publicly.
- Are there emotional factors that may affect decision-making? Reputation damage is deeply personal, and clients in distress may have unrealistic expectations or resist necessary timelines.

### Step 2: Reputation Audit

Once the initial facts are established, conduct a comprehensive reputation audit. See **A06 - Reputation Audit & Monitoring** for the full audit methodology and **H27 - Conducting a Reputation Audit** for the step-by-step process.

The audit for onboarding purposes should cover:

- **Full SERP mapping** for all relevant search terms (top 20 results each)
- **Review platform audit** across Google Reviews, Trustpilot, and industry-specific platforms
- **Social media presence audit** across all major platforms
- **Sentiment analysis** of the overall search landscape
- **Removability assessment**: For each negative result, an initial assessment of whether it can be removed, de-indexed, or must be suppressed

The audit typically takes 2-5 working days depending on complexity.

### Step 3: Strategy Recommendation

Based on the audit findings, develop a strategy recommendation that combines the appropriate action guides. The strategy should address:

**What can be removed?**
Identify content that is eligible for removal through RTBF, platform reporting, legal takedown, or direct contact. See **A01 - Content Removal & De-indexing**. For each removable item, estimate the likelihood of success and the timeline.

**What needs to be suppressed?**
For content that cannot be removed, map out a suppression strategy using positive content creation, social media optimization, and technical SEO. See **A02 - Positive Content Creation & SEO Suppression**.

**What else is needed?**
Depending on the scenario, the strategy may also include:
- Crisis communications (see **A03**) if the situation is active
- Review management (see **A04**) if reviews are part of the problem
- Social media optimization (see **A05**) to build controlled search result assets
- Legal escalation (see **A07**) if content is clearly unlawful
- Thought leadership (see **A08**) for executives and public figures
- Technical SEO (see **A09**) to build the foundation for all content to perform

**Phased approach:**
Present the strategy in phases rather than as a single overwhelming plan. Typically:

- Phase 1 (Months 1-3): Foundation — audit, quick wins (easy removals, social profile setup), website optimization
- Phase 2 (Months 3-6): Active campaign — content creation, PR placements, review management, ongoing removal efforts
- Phase 3 (Months 6-12): Consolidation — continued content, monitoring, strategy adjustment based on results
- Phase 4 (Ongoing): Maintenance — monitoring, periodic content publication, review management

### Step 4: Timeline and Budget Agreement

Present the strategy with clear timelines and costs. Key principles:

**Be honest about timelines.** Reputation management is slow. SEO changes take months to manifest. Removal requests have unpredictable timelines. Under-promising and over-delivering is always better than the reverse.

Typical timelines to communicate:

- Quick wins (profile optimization, easy removals): 2-4 weeks
- Measurable SERP improvement: 3-6 months
- Comprehensive reputation transformation: 6-18 months
- Ongoing maintenance: indefinite

**Budget transparency.** Break down costs by activity so the client understands what they are paying for. Common cost components:

- Audit and strategy development (one-off)
- Content creation (per piece or monthly retainer)
- PR placement (per placement or monthly retainer)
- SEO and technical work (monthly retainer)
- Legal costs (if applicable, typically passed through)
- Monitoring and reporting (monthly retainer)
- Review management (monthly retainer)

**Manage expectations explicitly.** State in writing what is and is not achievable. For example: "We expect to push the [specific negative result] to Page 2 within 6 months. We cannot guarantee complete removal of this content because it is published by a major news outlet." Clear expectation-setting prevents disputes later.

### Step 5: Kick-Off

Once the client agrees to the strategy and budget, conduct a formal kick-off meeting to:

1. **Confirm the strategy and priorities** — ensure everyone is aligned on what happens first
2. **Assign responsibilities** — who on the team owns each workstream?
3. **Establish communication cadence** — weekly calls, monthly reports, or quarterly reviews?
4. **Collect assets** — access to the client's website, social media credentials, any existing content, professional photography, biographical information
5. **Set up monitoring** — implement the monitoring framework from day one (see **H28 - Setting Up Reputation Monitoring**)
6. **Define success metrics** — agree on the KPIs that will be used to evaluate progress

## Key Deliverable: Initial Assessment Report

The centrepiece of the onboarding process is the initial assessment report. This is the document that translates the audit findings into a clear, actionable strategy for the client.

The report should include:

- **Executive summary**: A one-page overview of the current situation and recommended approach
- **Current state**: Detailed SERP maps, review summaries, social media audit, sentiment analysis
- **Risk assessment**: What could get worse if no action is taken?
- **Recommended strategy**: Prioritised list of actions with rationale
- **Timeline**: Realistic milestones over the campaign period
- **Budget**: Detailed cost breakdown
- **Success metrics**: How progress will be measured
- **Assumptions and limitations**: What the strategy cannot achieve and why

This report serves as the reference document throughout the engagement. It is what the client approved, what the team executes against, and what progress is measured against.

## Setting Realistic Expectations

The most important function of onboarding is expectation management. Common misconceptions to address:

- **"Can you remove it by next week?"** — Most removal processes take weeks to months. Set realistic timeframes from the start.
- **"I want it completely gone from the internet"** — Complete removal is often impossible. Explain the difference between removal and de-indexing, and between de-indexing and suppression.
- **"How much will it cost?"** — Reputation management is an ongoing investment, not a one-off fix. Frame costs accordingly.
- **"Will it definitely work?"** — No ethical agency can guarantee specific outcomes. Explain what is likely, what is possible, and what is unlikely.
- **"I just need SEO"** — Clients often underestimate the complexity. Explain why a multi-channel approach is more effective than any single tactic.

## Combining With Other Action Guides

Client onboarding is the gateway to all other strategies. The onboarding process determines which combination of the following will be deployed:

- **A01**: Content removal — if removable content is identified in the audit
- **A02**: SEO suppression — for content that cannot be removed
- **A03**: Crisis communications — if the situation is time-critical
- **A04**: Review management — if reviews are a significant factor
- **A05**: Social media optimization — for almost every engagement
- **A06**: Reputation audit and monitoring — the audit is the foundation of onboarding itself
- **A07**: Legal escalation — if legal routes are viable
- **A08**: Thought leadership — for executives and public figures
- **A09**: Technical SEO — for almost every engagement

See **H28 - Setting Up Reputation Monitoring** for the technical setup that begins immediately after kick-off.
