# A04 - Review Management & Response

## Overview

Online reviews are one of the most visible and influential elements of a business's reputation. A single star improvement on Google Reviews can increase revenue by 5-9%. Conversely, a pattern of negative reviews — or even a single highly visible one — can deter potential customers before they ever engage with the business.

Review management is not simply about responding to negative reviews. It is a comprehensive strategy that encompasses generating positive reviews from satisfied customers, responding professionally to all reviews (positive and negative), flagging fraudulent or policy-violating reviews for removal, and intercepting complaints before they become public reviews. This guide provides the strategic framework for managing reviews across platforms.

## Applicable Scenarios

- **S06 - Negative business reviews dominating search**: When poor reviews are the primary reputation problem
- **S09 - Corporate crisis aftermath**: When a crisis has triggered a wave of negative reviews
- **S10 - Fraudulent or fake reviews**: When competitors or bad actors are posting false reviews

## The Four Pillars of Review Management

### Pillar 1: Respond to All Reviews

Every review deserves a response. This signals to both the reviewer and future readers that the business is attentive, professional, and cares about customer experience.

**Responding to positive reviews:**
- Thank the reviewer by name where possible
- Reference a specific detail from their review to show genuine engagement
- Keep it brief — 2-3 sentences is sufficient
- Avoid marketing language or upselling in responses

**Responding to negative reviews:**
- Respond within 24-48 hours — speed signals that you take feedback seriously
- Acknowledge the customer's experience without being defensive
- Apologise for their dissatisfaction (this is not admitting fault — it is showing empathy)
- Take the conversation offline: "Please contact us at [email] so we can resolve this directly"
- Never argue, blame the customer, or question the validity of their experience publicly
- Follow up privately to resolve the issue, then politely ask if they would consider updating their review

See **H06 - Google Reviews Removal and Management** for platform-specific response guidance.

### Pillar 2: Generate Positive Reviews

The most effective way to improve a review profile is to increase the volume of positive reviews from genuinely satisfied customers. Most happy customers do not leave reviews unless prompted — the strategy is to make it easy and natural for them to do so.

**Methods for generating reviews:**

- **Post-service email sequences**: Send a follow-up email after a positive interaction asking for a review, with a direct link to the review platform
- **SMS review requests**: Text messages have higher open rates than email and work well for service businesses
- **In-person requests**: Train staff to ask satisfied customers to leave a review at the point of service
- **QR codes**: Place QR codes linking to review platforms on receipts, business cards, packaging, or in-store signage
- **Review landing pages**: Create a page on the company website that links to all relevant review platforms

**Important compliance notes:**
- Never offer incentives for reviews (this violates the terms of every major review platform and is illegal under UK Consumer Protection from Unfair Trading Regulations 2008)
- Never ask for specifically positive reviews — ask for honest feedback
- Never create fake reviews or use review farms
- Timing matters — ask when satisfaction is highest, typically immediately after service delivery

### Pillar 3: Flag Fraudulent Reviews

Fake reviews are a genuine problem, whether they come from competitors, disgruntled ex-employees, or organised review manipulation campaigns. Each major platform has mechanisms for reporting reviews that violate their policies.

**Grounds for flagging reviews:**

- The reviewer was never a customer (no record of the transaction)
- The review contains factually false claims
- The review is for the wrong business
- The review contains hate speech, threats, or discriminatory language
- The review is part of a coordinated campaign (multiple reviews from similar accounts in a short period)
- The review violates the platform's specific content policies

See **H06 - Google Reviews Removal and Management** for the Google flagging process and **H26 - Review Management Strategy** for multi-platform approaches.

**Evidence to collect before flagging:**
- Screenshots of the review
- Internal records showing no matching customer
- Patterns suggesting coordination (similar language, timing, account age)
- Any communication from the reviewer that reveals their identity or motive

### Pillar 4: Intercept Complaints

The best review management is preventing negative reviews from being posted in the first place. This means creating systems that capture dissatisfied customers before they turn to public review platforms.

**Interception strategies:**

- **Internal feedback mechanisms**: Provide easy ways for customers to complain directly — feedback forms, dedicated email addresses, customer service chat
- **Satisfaction surveys**: Send post-service surveys that identify unhappy customers. Route negative survey responses to a recovery team immediately.
- **Complaint resolution SLAs**: Commit to responding to all complaints within a defined timeframe (e.g., 4 hours during business hours)
- **Service recovery protocols**: Train staff in service recovery — acknowledging the problem, apologising, offering a remedy, and following up
- **Social media monitoring**: Watch for complaints on social media that could escalate to formal reviews if not addressed

## Key Metrics to Track

Effective review management requires consistent measurement. Track these metrics monthly:

- **Average star rating** on each platform (the headline number)
- **Total review count** on each platform (volume builds credibility)
- **Response rate** — what percentage of reviews receive a response (target: 100%)
- **Response time** — average time between a review being posted and a response being published (target: under 48 hours)
- **Sentiment trend** — is the overall sentiment improving, stable, or declining over time?
- **Review velocity** — how many new reviews are being received per week/month?
- **Negative review percentage** — what proportion of new reviews are 1-2 stars?

See **H28 - Setting Up Reputation Monitoring** for tools and dashboards to track these metrics.

## Platform Priorities

Not all review platforms are equally important. Prioritise based on:

### Google Reviews (Highest Priority)

Google Reviews appear directly in search results, in the knowledge panel, and on Google Maps. They are the most visible review platform for almost every business. A strong Google Reviews profile (4.0+ stars with 50+ reviews) is a powerful reputation asset. A poor one is immediately visible to anyone who searches for the business.

### Trustpilot

Trustpilot is the dominant review platform for e-commerce and service businesses in the UK and Europe. Trustpilot results frequently rank on Page 1 for company name searches, making it a critical platform to manage. See **H07** for Trustpilot-specific strategies.

### Industry-Specific Platforms

Depending on the client's industry, other platforms may be equally or more important:
- **TripAdvisor**: Hospitality, restaurants, tourism (see **H08**)
- **Glassdoor**: Employer reputation
- **Checkatrade / Rated People**: Tradespeople
- **Doctors.net / NHS Choices**: Healthcare
- **Solicitors Regulation Authority**: Legal services
- **Feefo**: E-commerce (invited reviews only)

## Combining Review Management With Other Strategies

Review management works most effectively when integrated with:

- **Content removal (A01)**: For reviews that are clearly fake, defamatory, or policy-violating, pursue removal through platform mechanisms
- **SEO suppression (A02)**: If review aggregation pages rank highly for the business name, positive content can push them down
- **Crisis communications (A03)**: During a crisis, review management is critical — a flood of angry reviews can persist long after the crisis itself has passed
- **Social media management (A05)**: Social media complaints and reviews are closely linked — monitor both channels together
- **Reputation monitoring (A06)**: Continuous monitoring catches new reviews quickly, enabling fast responses

## Timeline for Results

Review management is a continuous process, but expect the following milestones:

- **Weeks 1-2**: Respond to all existing unanswered reviews. Flag clearly fraudulent reviews for removal.
- **Weeks 2-4**: Implement review generation systems (email sequences, QR codes, staff training).
- **Months 1-3**: New positive reviews begin shifting the average rating upward. Response rate reaches 100%.
- **Months 3-6**: Consistent review generation produces measurable improvement in average rating and total review count.
- **Months 6-12**: The review profile is significantly stronger. Negative reviews are outnumbered by positive ones, and the average rating reflects genuine customer satisfaction.

## When Review Management Alone Is Not Enough

If the underlying service or product quality is poor, review management cannot fix the problem. Reviews are a reflection of reality — managing them is about ensuring that reflection is fair and representative, not about manufacturing a false impression. If negative reviews are consistently accurate, the priority must be fixing the operational issues that cause them.
