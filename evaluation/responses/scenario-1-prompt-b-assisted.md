## 1. Detailed strategy to transform her search results

## A. Start with a forensic audit in week 1

Create a spreadsheet of:

- all Page 1-3 Google web results for exact-name and name-plus-role searches;
- all Google Images results for her name;
- every image source page, image file URL, licence library listing, caption, and thumbnail;

  - incomplete because it omits the outcome/resolution;
  - still obviously in the public interest;
  - merely historic and disproportionate now.

This is consistent with the immediate assessment workflow in [S01] and the “remove first where viable, suppress in parallel” framework in [A01]. 

### Triage the results into 4 buckets

**Bucket 1 — strongest removal prospects**
- duplicate or syndicated copies;
- trade press republications;
- articles with factual inaccuracies, misleading headlines, or omitted resolution;
- image-library pages with weak editorial value;
- old cached thumbnails after source deletion.

**Bucket 2 — moderate prospects**
- mainstream articles that are accurate but stale, excessive, or no longer relevant when searched by name;
- image pages where the photo is not needed for ongoing public interest.

**Bucket 3 — weak removal prospects**
- BBC / major national reporting that is accurate and still framed as a matter of governance or public accountability;
- articles still prominent because her online footprint is thin.

**Bucket 4 — suppression only**
- URLs likely to survive all formal challenges and need to be displaced by stronger positive assets.

## B. Run removal and suppression at the same time

Your internal guide is explicit that removal should be attempted first where viable, but **positive content creation should begin immediately rather than waiting for legal outcomes** [A01]. The tribunal case study also shows that **image removal and personal brand rebuilding must run together** [CS02]. 

### Workstream 1: source-level article action
Target the publisher first. Google itself says it can only remove from Search; if the article or image stays live, the best result still comes from source removal, correction, or noindex. ([support.google.com](https://support.google.com/websearch/answer/9673730?hl=en&utm_source=chatgpt.com))

For each article, seek one of these outcomes in order:

1. **full removal**;
2. **noindex** on the article page;
3. **headline / intro amendment**;
4. **correction or clarification**;
5. **addition of the final outcome / resolution / her statement**;
6. **comment removal** if comments exist.

That ladder mirrors [H08].

## 2. Specific steps to remove or de-index articles and court images

## A. Google RTBF: how to frame this case

Her argument is strongest if positioned as:

- **historic employment litigation from 3 years ago**;
- **not a current office-holder search with immediate public-interest urgency**;
- **search results now present an excessive, one-dimensional picture**;
- **the ranking is disproportionate to current relevance**;
- **the articles are incomplete if they omit outcome, context, later career, or absence of regulatory findings**.

The ICO’s delisting criteria expressly contemplate cases where **journalistic content may still warrant delisting** if the privacy impact now outweighs the benefit of name-search accessibility. The ICO also notes the right is not absolute, but it is available in appropriate cases. ([ico.org.uk](https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/search-result-delisting-criteria/?utm_source=chatgpt.com))

### RTBF submission template for a news article

```text
Subject: Request for delisting under UK GDPR / European privacy law

I request delisting of the below URL from Google search results returned for searches of my name.

URL: [insert]
Search query: “[full name]”

This article concerns an employment tribunal matter from [month/year], now approximately three years old. While it may have been reportable at the time, its continued prominence in search results for my name is now no longer relevant, excessive, and disproportionate in relation to my current private and professional life.

The result creates an incomplete and misleading impression because it presents a historic dispute as the defining feature of my identity. It does not fairly reflect subsequent developments, my current activities, or the limited present-day public interest in this matter.

I am not seeking to erase the historical record at source. I am requesting delisting from name-based searches because the ongoing privacy and reputational harm caused by this result outweighs any continuing public interest in making it prominently accessible against my name.

This result is having a direct adverse impact on professional opportunities, including board-level applications, despite the age and limited current relevance of the underlying matter.

I therefore ask that this URL be delisted for searches of my name on applicable Google services.
```

This tracks the argument structure in [H01].t improve odds
Attach:
- proof of identity;
- current CV/board bio;
- evidence of current role focus;
- evidence the matter is historic;
- evidence of inaccuracy, omission, or resolved context where available;
- a short statement explaining current harm.

[H01] and [H02] are right that documentation and follow-up responses materially affect outcomes.

### If Google rejects the first request
Appeal / resubmit with stronger evidence. [H02] recommends explicitly addressing the rejection reason, especially if Google says the article “relates to your current profession” or remains of “substantial public interest.” It also notes that **submitting via the individual’s own email rather than an agency address can help** in some cases [H02]. 

#### RTBF appeal template

```text
Subject: Resubmission / appeal of delisting request

Thank you for reviewing my prior request. I understand the concern that the content may have been considered relevant to my professional life. I ask that the request be reconsidered in light of the following additional information.

The article relates to a historic employment dispute from [year]. It is not an ongoing matter and is not indicative of my current professional activities. Its continued prominence in search results for my name now presents a disproportionate and outdated picture of me.

Since publication, my circumstances have materially changed:
- [insert changed role / current NED focus]
- [insert absence of repeat issues / resolution / clarification]
- [insert evidence attached]

The result is no longer relevant in the way it may once have been, and its privacy impact is significant. I therefore ask Google to reassess the balance between current public interest and the ongoing harm caused by prominent name-based indexing.
```

## C. Regulatory and complaint routes by outlet

### Daily Mail
The Daily Mail is within IPSO’s regulatory umbrella. IPSO handles complaints under the Editors’ Code, including **Clause 1 (Accuracy)** and issues involving online comments; IPSO says complaints generally need to be within its time rules and it can require corrections or adjudications, though not article deletion. ([ipso.co.uk](https://www.ipso.co.uk/who-ipso-regulates/daily-mail-associated-newspapers-limited/?utm_source=chatgpt.com))

### BBC
BBC complaints follow the BBC’s own complaints framework first. That is a separate route from IPSO. ([downloads.bbc.co.uk](https://downloads.bbc.co.uk/aboutthebbc/insidethebbc/howwework/accountability/consultations/complaints_framework.pdf?utm_source=chatgpt.com))

### Guardian
The Guardian has its own corrections/complaints mechanisms and is not a straightforward “send everything to IPSO” play in the same way as the Mail. In practice, approach through editorial complaints / readers’ editor style routes first, not a one-size-fits-all regulator tactic. ([theguardian.com](https://www.theguardian.com/news/2026/apr/03/corrections-and-clarifications?utm_source=chatgpt.com))

### Important limitation
IPSO’s complaints route is useful mainly for **correction / accuracy / privacy leverage**, not for forcing deletion. Your internal guide is correct: for old archive content, the more realistic asks are often **correction, update, comments removal, or noindex** [H08].

## 4. Legal routes under UK / EU law

## A. Google delisting under UK/EU privacy law
This is the primary legal route for search suppression of lawful but outdated reporting. Google provides a dedicated form for European privacy-law delisting, and says it assesses whether content is **inadequate, irrelevant, no longer relevant, or excessive**, balanced against public interest. ([reportcontent.google.com](https://reportcontent.google.com/forms/rtbf?utm_source=chatgpt.com))

## B. Right to erasure / data rights against publishers
Under UK GDPR, individuals can seek erasure in certain circumstances, but the right is **not absolute** and organisations must respond without undue delay and usually within one month. The key problem here is that **journalism exemptions** can limit that route for publishers. ([ico.org.uk](https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/uk-gdpr-guidance-and-resources/individual-rights/individual-rights/right-to-erasure/?utm_source=chatgpt.com))

Practical consequence:  
- use UK GDPR / erasure arguments as leverage with image libraries and archives;
- expect mainstream publishers to rely on journalism/freedom-of-expression grounds more often than search engines.

## C. ICO complaint against Google or another controller
If Google refuses delisting, [H02] correctly identifies the **ICO complaint** route. The ICO expressly says search engines must consider name-based delisting requests, and it publishes criteria on when journalistic content may still warrant delisting. ([ico.org.uk](https://ico.org.uk/for-the-public/online/internet-search-results/?utm_source=chatgpt.com))

Use the ICO where:
- Google rejects despite age, limited current relevance, and strong privacy harm;
- you have a clean evidence pack;
- the articles are now disproportionate in branded search.

## D. IPSO / BBC complaints / publisher standards complaints
Use these where there is:
- inaccuracy;
- misleading omission;
- privacy intrusion;
- harmful reader comments;
- refusal to reflect an outcome or clarification.

IPSO can require corrections/adjudications, but not deletion. BBC has its own framework first. ([ipso.co.uk](https://www.ipso.co.uk/making-a-complaint/?utm_source=chatgpt.com))

## E. Defamation / malicious falsehood / privacy misuse
Only use this if there is a **strong merits case**:
- false factual allegations;
- defamatory sting;
- serious harm;
- misleading headline that materially distorts the position.

Your internal materials are right to warn about the **Streisand effect** and the need to use legal escalation carefully [A01], [S01], [H08]. 

## F. Territorial limit
Even if Google grants delisting, it may not be global. The CJEU’s **Google v CNIL** ruling confirmed that EU-style delisting is not automatically worldwide. In practice, the win is strongest on applicable European services and geographies, which is still highly relevant for a UK-based board-search problem. ([eur-lex.europa.eu](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/en/TXT/?uri=CELEX%3A62017CJ0507&utm_source=chatgpt.com))
