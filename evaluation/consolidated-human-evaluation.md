# Consolidated Human Evaluation

## Methodology
- **Evaluators:** Andrew, Ben, Charlie, Jen (4 reviewers)
- **Model tested:** GPT 5.3 Thinking
- **Scoring:** 1-5 scale across 6 criteria (Relevance, Groundedness, Actionability, Risk Awareness, Accuracy, Trust)
- **Scenario 1:** 2 reviewers (Andrew, Charlie). Jen's feedback excluded due to an error in the prompt document she received (Scenario 1A contained the wrong prompt, which she correctly flagged). Ben did not review Scenario 1.
- **Scenarios 2 and 3:** 4 reviewers each (Andrew, Ben, Charlie, Jen)

## Consolidated Feedback by Scenario

### Scenario 1: UK Executive with Employment Tribunal Press Coverage

**Where Prompt B improved over A:**
- Groundedness was the clearest gain (+1.0): Prompt B's use of the CS02 case study (16 articles removed, 20 images removed, 4-month timeline) gave reviewers concrete evidence that Prompt A lacked. Andrew noted Prompt A had "no proprietary data or case validation."
- Risk Awareness and Accuracy both improved (+0.5 each): internal guidance reinforced realistic constraints and correct procedural application.

**Where Prompt B underperformed or drew mixed reactions:**
- Trust scored lower for Charlie (2 vs 3) despite scoring higher for Andrew (5 vs 3). Charlie flagged a specific formatting error — Prompt B has Workstream 1/2/4 but no 3 — noting "a simple mistake like this can discredit the response quite a bit; not as presentable to a client."
- Actionability was split: Andrew scored B higher (4 vs 3), but Charlie scored it lower (3.5 vs 4).

**Notable evaluator comments:**
- Charlie: "To answer directly 'Which answer would you actually use to advise a client?' then it would be prompt B. However that is because when advising I could hide some of the issues, and augment it with my own expertise. However if I was to deliver the answer verbatim to a client, or if I was an average user of the LLM and reading the answer directly, I would feel better about prompt A."
- Charlie: Prompt B's priority bucketing of results (quick wins vs longer-term goals) was a positive feature not present in A.
- Andrew: Prompt A was "legally sound and operationally correct" but lacked the nuance from real cases. Prompt B's trust score was "significantly higher due to real examples, metrics, and proven outcomes."

### Scenario 2: Restaurant Chain Facing Food Safety Crisis

**Where Prompt B improved over A:**
- Trust saw the largest gain (+1.50): the biggest single improvement across all scenarios. Prompt B's references to CS11 (food poisoning crisis with 352.5% Facebook recovery, 345% Twitter recovery) and CS04 (Trustpilot recovery from 5.4 to 7.0) gave it concrete proof points.
- Groundedness improved (+0.88) through case study references and internal playbook citations.
- Accuracy improved (+0.50) as documented procedures reduced unsupported claims.

**Where Prompt B did not clearly improve:**
- Relevance dipped slightly (-0.25), though scores remained high (4.50 vs 4.75).
- Risk Awareness was unchanged on average (3.75). Charlie noted both prompts "state what not to do, but don't delve into what will go wrong if the incorrect steps are taken."
- Ben scored both prompts identically across all criteria, finding the differences minimal.

**Notable evaluator comments:**
- Charlie: "I've gone for 2 [on Trust for A] because it references CDC/CERC and gov websites, and it reads as trustworthy. However, based off of your definition for Trust (References real examples and proven outcomes to build confidence) I can't go higher, and 2 might be slightly generous. Even a section at the bottom 'Previous examples of this strategy in action' displaying case studies and the results would improve this score immensely."
- Charlie: On Prompt B's Groundedness: "Put 5 here because of the extensive referencing to case studies. To caveat it though; we have supplied the case studies here? Is the end goal that a user could get that type of response without having to supply case studies they have found themselves?"
- Charlie: "I would be more inclined to use prompt B for a client, as the substance of it was better. I did think prompt A got a better response in terms of readability and ease of being able to follow it."
- Ben: Prices seem high for our agency. Both prompts could do with advice on generating positive reviews as a counter strategy.
- Jen: "Both are good options honestly. A does a good job, but B feels more structured perhaps. The difference isn't huge though."

### Scenario 3: Company Facing Defamatory Forum Posts and Negative Reviews

**Where Prompt B improved over A:**
- Trust saw the largest gain (+1.88): the biggest single improvement in the entire evaluation. The CS07 case study (45% to 20% negative reduction) and documented escalation procedures from H04 gave reviewers confidence.
- Groundedness improved strongly (+1.38) through case study metrics and procedural guide citations.
- Actionability improved (+0.75) with clearer execution roadmaps grounded in proven methods.

**Where Prompt B could still improve:**
- Risk Awareness improved only modestly (+0.38) and remains the weakest criterion for both prompts. Charlie scored it low for both (2 and 2.5).

**Notable evaluator comments:**

## Key Themes Across All Scenarios

### 1. Trust and Groundedness are the strongest differentiators
The two criteria with the largest average improvements from A to B were Trust (+1.33) and Groundedness (+1.10). Across all three scenarios, every reviewer consistently scored Prompt A low on these criteria due to the absence of case studies and proven outcomes. Prompt B's citation of specific metrics (e.g. CS02's 4-month timeline, CS11's social media recovery percentages, CS07's 45% to 20% negative reduction) was the single most impactful difference.

### 2. Relevance is already strong in baseline prompts
Relevance was the highest-scoring criterion for Prompt A (4.45 average) and saw no meaningful improvement in Prompt B (-0.10). The model addresses the right issues regardless of whether context is provided.

### 3. Risk Awareness is the weakest criterion for both prompts
Risk Awareness showed the smallest improvement (+0.27) and was flagged by multiple reviewers as an area where both prompts underperform. Charlie specifically noted the responses "state what not to do, but don't delve into what will go wrong if the incorrect steps are taken."

### 4. Readability vs substance trade-off
Charlie raised a recurring observation: Prompt A tended to be more readable and easier to follow, while Prompt B had better substance but occasionally suffered from formatting errors (e.g. missing Workstream 3 numbering) that could undermine credibility if delivered directly to a client.

### 5. Actionability needs more step-by-step detail
Multiple reviewers (Ben, Charlie) noted that both prompts could be more specific in their actionable guidance. Charlie commented that to score Actionability as 5/5, responses would need to "really spell it out for people and take them through every process." Ben wanted specific publication names and profile suggestions.

### 6. Budget figures need grounding
Ben flagged that the suggested budgets seem high for a typical agency. Charlie noted that Prompt A listed prices "without sources." Budget ranges in responses should ideally be tied to documented precedent or clearly labelled as indicative.

### 7. Questions about scalability of the RAG approach
Charlie raised a strategic question about the evaluation methodology itself: "We have supplied the case studies here. Is the end goal that a user could get that type of response without having to supply case studies they have found themselves?" This highlights the need to clarify the intended delivery mechanism for the archive-assisted approach.
